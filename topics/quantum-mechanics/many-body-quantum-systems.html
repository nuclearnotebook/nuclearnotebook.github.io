Second Quantisation, the Hubbard Model, and Quantum Phase Transitions

Why We Need Second Quantisation

For N identical particles, the antisymmetric N-particle wave function (Slater determinant for fermions) is an N×N determinant — unwieldy for large N and awkward when particle number changes. Second quantisation reformulates quantum mechanics entirely in terms of field operators acting on Fock space, making particle creation, annihilation, and the statistics automatically built in.

Fock Spaceℱ = ℋ₀ ⊕ ℋ₁ ⊕ ℋ₂ ⊕ ...     (vacuum ⊕ 1-particle ⊕ 2-particle ⊕ ...)

Fermionic creation/annihilation operators {ĉᵢ, ĉⱼ†} = δᵢⱼ automatically enforce Pauli exclusion: (ĉᵢ†)² = 0 (cannot create two fermions in same state). Bosonic operators [âᵢ, âⱼ†] = δᵢⱼ allow unlimited occupation.

The Hubbard Model

Hubbard HamiltonianĤ = −t Σ_{⟨ij⟩,σ} (ĉ†ᵢσĉⱼσ + h.c.) + U Σᵢ n̂ᵢ↑n̂ᵢ↓

The kinetic term (hopping t) favours delocalised, metallic electrons. The interaction term (on-site repulsion U) favours localised, insulating configurations. At half-filling (one electron per site on average), the competition produces the Mott metal-insulator transition at U/t ~ 4-6 (in 3D).

Quantum Phase Transitions

At T=0, quantum fluctuations (not thermal fluctuations) drive phase transitions as a non-thermal control parameter (U/t, magnetic field B, pressure P) is tuned through a critical value. At the quantum critical point (QCP), the correlation length ξ and correlation time ξ_τ both diverge: ξ ∝ |δ|^{−ν}, ξ_τ ∝ ξ^z, where δ is the distance from criticality and z is the dynamical critical exponent.

The quantum critical point influences physical properties not just at T=0 but in a "quantum critical fan" extending to finite temperature above the QCP. In this region, thermodynamic and transport properties show non-Fermi-liquid behaviour (resistivity ρ ∝ T rather than T²), strongly enhanced effective masses, and anomalous spectral functions — observed in cuprates, heavy fermion metals, and iron-based superconductors.

🌡 High-Temperature Superconductivity

Cuprate high-temperature superconductors (discovered 1986, T_c up to 135 K) are doped Mott insulators — start from an antiferromagnetic Mott insulator and add holes by chemical substitution. The resulting phase diagram (pseudogap, strange metal, d-wave superconductor, Fermi liquid) emerges from the Hubbard model at finite doping. The pairing mechanism — phonon exchange (BCS), spin fluctuations (spin-wave glue), or resonating valence bonds (Anderson) — remains actively debated. Understanding high-T_c superconductivity is one of the most important unsolved problems in condensed matter physics, with implications for lossless power transmission, maglev trains, and quantum computing hardware.
