Three Statistics — Three Different Physical Worlds

Identical particles come in two types — fermions and bosons — with fundamentally different statistical behaviour that shapes the entire physical world. Classical Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics (treating particles as distinguishable) gives way to:

  • Fermi-Dirac statistics: Electrons, protons, neutrons, quarks — maximum one particle per state
  • Bose-Einstein statistics: Photons, ⁴He, Cooper pairs, ⁸⁷Rb atoms — unlimited particles per state, with bunching tendency

These are not merely theoretical curiosities — they govern the behaviour of all matter at low temperatures, explaining metals, semiconductors, superfluids, superconductors, and Bose-Einstein condensates.

Fermi-Dirac Distribution — Electrons

Fermi-Dirac Occupation Probabilityf_FD(E) = 1 / (exp[(E−μ)/(k_BT)] + 1)     0 ≤ f_FD ≤ 1

At T=0: f_FD(E) = 1 for E < μ = E_F (all states filled), 0 for E > E_F (all states empty). At finite T, the sharp step broadens over ~4k_BT. The chemical potential μ (Fermi level) at T=0 equals the Fermi energy E_F. For a free electron gas of density n:

Fermi Energy (3D free electron gas)E_F = ℏ²(3π²n)^{2/3} / (2mₑ)

Bose-Einstein Distribution and Condensation

Bose-Einstein Occupation Numberf_BE(E) = 1 / (exp[(E−μ)/(k_BT)] − 1)     f_BE ≥ 0, diverges as E → μ

For bosons, the chemical potential must satisfy μ ≤ E_min to keep f_BE ≥ 0. As T decreases, μ approaches E_0 (ground state energy). At the critical temperature T_BEC, μ reaches E_0 and the ground state population diverges — Bose-Einstein condensation:

BEC Critical TemperatureT_BEC = 3.31ℏ²n^{2/3} / (mk_B)     N₀/N = 1 − (T/T_BEC)^{3/2}

The first gaseous BEC was achieved by Cornell, Wieman (JILA, 1995) and Ketterle (MIT, 1995) using laser-cooled and magnetically trapped ⁸⁷Rb atoms at T ≈ 170 nK — winning the 2001 Nobel Prize. The condensate can be observed directly with absorption imaging and behaves as a coherent matter wave, showing interference fringes when two condensates overlap.

🌟 Planck's Law from Bose-Einstein Statistics

The blackbody radiation law I(ν,T) = (2hν³/c²)/(e^{hν/kT}−1) is the Bose-Einstein distribution for photons with μ=0 (photon number not conserved by emission/absorption). The denominator e^{hν/kT}−1 is purely bosonic — without it, the distribution would be the classical Rayleigh-Jeans law I ∝ ν²T, which diverges at high frequencies (the ultraviolet catastrophe). The bosonic denominator introduces the factor 1/(e^{hν/kT}−1) which suppresses high-frequency modes exponentially. Planck's discovery of this formula in 1900 launched quantum mechanics — the first quantisation postulate was required precisely to explain this Bose-Einstein factor.
