The One-Time Pad — Unconditional Security

Claude Shannon proved in 1949 that the one-time pad (XOR of plaintext with a truly random key as long as the message, used exactly once and destroyed) is information-theoretically secure — unbreakable regardless of the attacker's computational power, even with unlimited computing resources including quantum computers. The single practical obstacle: how to distribute a long random key secretly to two distant parties. Classical public-key cryptography (RSA, Diffie-Hellman) solves this computationally — but Shor's quantum algorithm breaks these in polynomial time on a quantum computer. Quantum key distribution solves key distribution with security guaranteed by physical law.

BB84 Protocol — Step by Step

Charles Bennett and Gilles Brassard devised BB84 (1984), the first practical quantum cryptographic protocol. It uses four polarisation states in two conjugate bases:

BB84 State SetBasis + (rectilinear): |→⟩ = bit 0, |↑⟩ = bit 1
Basis × (diagonal): |↗⟩ = bit 0, |↖⟩ = bit 1

The protocol proceeds in four phases:

  • Quantum transmission: Alice sends N photons, each in a randomly chosen state from the four BB84 states. Bob measures each in a randomly chosen basis (+ or ×).
  • Basis reconciliation: Alice and Bob publicly compare which basis they used for each photon (but NOT the bit values). They keep only positions where they used the same basis (~50%) — the "sifted key."
  • Error estimation: They publicly compare a random sample (~10%) of sifted bits to estimate the Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER). If QBER > ~11%, they abort (Eve detected); otherwise they discard the sample.
  • Key distillation: Error correction and privacy amplification reduce the remaining sifted key to a shorter, provably secret key, compressing out any information Eve might have gained.

Why Eavesdropping Is Detectable

The physical security of BB84 rests on two quantum mechanical principles:

No-cloning theorem: An unknown quantum state cannot be perfectly copied. Any eavesdropping strategy that copies Alice's photons to keep information for later must disturb the original — measurement collapses the quantum state.

Heisenberg uncertainty: Measuring a photon in the wrong basis (which Eve must do ~50% of the time, not knowing Alice's basis) destroys the original state. When Bob measures Eve's re-sent photon in the correct basis, he gets the wrong answer ~25% of the time. This introduces QBER ≈ 25% in Eve's intercept-resend attack — far above the security threshold of ~11%.

Unconditional Security Proofs

The security of BB84 has been proven rigorously: Mayers (1996), Lo-Chau (1999), and Shor-Preskill (2000) proved that after privacy amplification, the final key is secure against any eavesdropping strategy allowed by quantum mechanics — including collective attacks, coherent attacks, and side-channel attacks on the quantum channel. The secret key rate:

Shor-Preskill Secret Key RateR ≥ 1 − H(e) − H(e) = 1 − 2H(e)     [H(e) = binary entropy of bit error rate e]

Positive key rate requires e < 11.0% (the QKD threshold). Above this error rate, Eve could have full information about the sifted key and the protocol is aborted.

🔒 Post-Quantum Cryptography

NIST finalised post-quantum cryptographic standards in August 2024: CRYSTALS-Kyber (ML-KEM) for key encapsulation and CRYSTALS-Dilithium (ML-DSA) for digital signatures, both based on lattice problems believed hard for quantum computers. These provide a software solution for "harvest now, decrypt later" attacks — adversaries recording encrypted traffic today to decrypt when quantum computers mature. QKD and post-quantum cryptography are complementary: QKD provides information-theoretic security for high-value real-time communications; post-quantum algorithms protect the broader internet infrastructure. Both are needed for a quantum-secure digital future.
