⚡ Relativistic Quantum Mechanics

The Dirac Equation, Antimatter, Spin from Relativity, and the Klein-Gordon Equation

The Incompatibility of Schrödinger and Special Relativity

The Schrödinger equation treats time and space fundamentally differently: it is first-order in time (∂/∂t) but second-order in space (∇²). Under a Lorentz boost, space and time mix — so a Lorentz-covariant wave equation must treat space and time on equal footing. For high-energy electrons (v → c), relativistic effects become crucial: relativistic corrections to hydrogen are ~α² ≈ 5×10⁻⁵ per term, large enough to observe as fine structure in spectroscopy. For heavy atoms (Z ≈ 80), relativistic corrections reach ~20% and must be included for correct predictions.

The Klein-Gordon Equation — First Attempt

The relativistic energy-momentum relation E² = (pc)² + (mc²)² suggests replacing E → iℏ∂/∂t and p → −iℏ∇:

Klein-Gordon Equation(□ + (mc/ℏ)²)ψ = 0     □ = ∂²/(c²∂t²) − ∇² (d'Alembertian)

This equation is Lorentz-covariant but has a fatal problem for a single-particle interpretation: the "probability density" ρ = (iℏ/2mc²)(ψ*∂ψ/∂t − ψ∂ψ*/∂t) can be negative — physically meaningless. The Klein-Gordon equation is correctly interpreted as a classical relativistic field equation (for scalar fields), with ψ becoming a quantum field operator. Its quanta describe spin-0 particles: pions, kaons, and the Higgs boson.

The Dirac Equation — Relativistic Wave Mechanics

Paul Dirac (1928) sought a relativistic wave equation first-order in both time and space derivatives, ensuring positive-definite probability density. To linearise E² = (pc)² + (mc²)², he introduced matrices:

Dirac Equation (covariant form)(iγ^μ∂_μ − mc/ℏ)ψ = 0     γ^μ = (β, βα⃗) — 4×4 Dirac matrices

The gamma matrices satisfy the Clifford algebra {γ^μ, γ^ν} = 2g^{μν}. The wave function ψ is a 4-component Dirac spinor. The 4 components correspond to: 2 spin states (up, down) × 2 energy signs (positive, negative).

Spin Emerges from Relativity

The most remarkable feature of the Dirac equation: spin-½ angular momentum is not an additional assumption — it emerges automatically from the requirement of Lorentz covariance. The orbital angular momentum L̂ does not separately commute with the Dirac Hamiltonian; only the total angular momentum J = L + ½ℏσ is conserved. The Dirac equation automatically predicts:

  • The electron has spin-½ with magnetic moment μ = g_e μ_B/2
  • The Dirac prediction: g_e = 2 exactly (compared to g = 1 for orbital motion)
  • QED correction: g_e = 2 + α/π − 0.328(α/π)² + ... ≈ 2.00231930436 (Schwinger 1948 + higher order)
  • Experimental: g_e = 2.00231930436146±0.00000000000053 (Harvard 2022) — agreement to 12 significant figures

Antimatter — The Dirac Sea and Modern Understanding

Dirac's equation has both positive (E > 0) and negative (E < 0) energy solutions. Dirac interpreted the negatives as: all negative-energy states are filled with electrons (the "Dirac sea"). A hole in this sea — a missing negative-energy, negative-charge electron — appears as a positive-energy, positive-charge particle: the positron. Predicted 1928; discovered by Carl Anderson in 1932 (Nobel Prize 1936).

The modern interpretation replaces the Dirac sea with quantum field theory: the negative-energy solutions describe positrons as independent quantum field excitations, not holes. The Dirac field ψ̂(x) creates electrons and annihilates positrons; ψ̂†(x) creates positrons and annihilates electrons. This quantum field theory of the Dirac equation combined with the photon field A^μ is quantum electrodynamics (QED).

⚧ PET Scanning — Dirac in Medicine

Positron Emission Tomography (PET) exploits Dirac's antimatter prediction medically. A radiotracer like ¹⁸F-fluorodeoxyglucose accumulates in metabolically active tissue (tumours, active brain regions). ¹⁸F beta-plus decays: ¹⁸F → ¹⁸O + e⁺ + ν_e. The emitted positron travels ~1 mm in tissue before annihilating with a tissue electron: e⁺ + e⁻ → 2γ (each 511 keV, back-to-back). Coincidence detection of both gamma rays within ~10 ns identifies a line through the body; tomographic reconstruction gives 3D metabolic activity maps. ~4 million PET scans annually in the US. The €700 million PET scanner market is built entirely on Paul Dirac's 1928 equation.
