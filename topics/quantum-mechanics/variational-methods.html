The Power of the Variational Principle

The variational principle provides a rigorous upper bound on the ground state energy without requiring exact solution. Its statement is deceptively simple: for any normalised trial wave function ψ_trial, the expectation value of the Hamiltonian is always ≥ the true ground state energy E₀:

Variational Principle (Ritz Method)E[ψ_trial] = ⟨ψ_trial|Ĥ|ψ_trial⟩ / ⟨ψ_trial|ψ_trial⟩ ≥ E₀     (equality iff ψ_trial = ψ₀)

Proof: expand ψ_trial = Σ cₙ|n⟩ in energy eigenstates. Then E[ψ_trial] = Σ |cₙ|² Eₙ ≥ E₀ Σ |cₙ|² = E₀. The inequality follows because each Eₙ ≥ E₀. The variational approach: choose a parameterised trial function, compute E as a function of parameters, minimise over parameters. Better trial functions → tighter upper bounds → more accurate energies.

Hydrogen Atom — Variational Benchmark

Using Gaussian trial function ψ_G = A·e^{−αr²} for hydrogen: computing ⟨T⟩ = 3ℏ²α/(2m) and ⟨V⟩ = −e²√(8α/π)/(4πε₀), minimising E(α) gives α_min = 8/(9πa₀²). The variational energy: E_var = −(8/3π)²·(13.6 eV) ≈ −11.5 eV vs exact −13.6 eV. Agreement to 85% with a single parameter. Using the exact 1s function ψ = e^{−r/a₀} (which is the exact solution), the variational calculation gives E = −13.6 eV exactly — the trial function is exact, so the bound is tight.

The Helium Atom — Two-Electron Problem

Helium has two electrons and the electron-electron repulsion e²/(4πε₀r₁₂) destroys exact solubility. Using a product of hydrogen-like 1s orbitals with effective nuclear charge Z* as the variational parameter:

Helium Trial Functionψ(r₁,r₂) = (Z*³/πa₀³) · e^{−Z*(r₁+r₂)/a₀}     (product of screened 1s orbitals)

Computing ⟨Ĥ⟩ and minimising: Z*_min = Z − 5/16 = 2 − 5/16 = 27/16 = 1.6875. Physical interpretation: each electron "screens" the nucleus from the other by an effective charge of 5/16 ≈ 0.31. Variational energy: E_var = −(Z*)² × 2 × 13.6 + (5/4)Z* × 13.6 = −77.5 eV. Experimental value: −79.0 eV. Error: 1.9% with just one variational parameter — a remarkable agreement for such a simple trial function.

Density Functional Theory — Variational at Scale

Walter Kohn and Lu Jeu Sham (1965) reformulated the many-body problem using the variational principle with electron density n(r) as the fundamental variable:

Kohn-Sham DFT Energy FunctionalE[n] = T_s[n] + ∫V_ext(r)n(r)d³r + E_Hartree[n] + E_xc[n]

Where T_s[n] is the non-interacting kinetic energy, E_Hartree is the classical Coulomb repulsion, and E_xc[n] is the exchange-correlation energy (the quantum many-body correction, approximated as a functional of n(r)). Minimising δE/δn = 0 subject to ∫n(r)d³r = N yields the Kohn-Sham equations — N coupled single-particle Schrödinger equations with an effective potential V_KS[n].

🧩 Quantum Chemistry in Drug Discovery

Modern drug discovery uses variational quantum chemistry at every stage: DFT computes binding affinities between drug candidates and protein targets, identifies likely metabolic pathways (CYP450 reactions), predicts NMR chemical shifts for structure verification, and optimises molecular geometry for ADMET (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, toxicity) properties. Machine learning potentials trained on DFT data (like ANI, DeePMD, MACE) extend first-principles accuracy to million-atom simulations of protein folding and membrane dynamics. AlphaFold2's energy function incorporates DFT-derived bonding terms calibrated variationally on crystallographic data.
