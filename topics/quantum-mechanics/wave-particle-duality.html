The Crisis at the Turn of the 20th Century

By 1900, classical physics faced two profound experimental puzzles that its equations could not resolve. The first was blackbody radiation — the spectrum of light emitted by a hot object. Classical theory (the Rayleigh-Jeans law) predicted that the radiated power at high frequencies should diverge to infinity — the "ultraviolet catastrophe." The second was the photoelectric effect: ultraviolet light striking a metal surface ejects electrons, but only if the frequency exceeds a threshold value, regardless of intensity — directly contradicting classical wave theory.

Max Planck resolved the first in 1900 by postulating that electromagnetic energy is emitted in discrete quanta: E = hf, where h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s is Planck's constant. Albert Einstein resolved the second in 1905 — his Nobel Prize work — by taking Planck's quanta literally: light consists of discrete photons, each carrying energy E = hf. If hf is less than the metal's work function, no individual photon has enough energy to eject an electron, regardless of how many photons (intensity) arrive.

The Photoelectric Effect — Light as Particles

Einstein's analysis of the photoelectric effect provided the first compelling evidence that light behaves as particles (photons) in certain circumstances:

Photoelectric EquationKEmax = hf − φ     [φ = work function of metal, in eV]

Maximum kinetic energy of ejected electrons is determined solely by the photon frequency f, not the light intensity. Increasing intensity increases the number of ejected electrons (more photons) but not their energy. The threshold frequency f₀ = φ/h: below this, no electrons are ejected no matter how intense the light.

This directly contradicted the classical wave model, in which energy delivery rate (intensity) should determine ejection energy. Millikan spent a decade trying to disprove Einstein's equation — and confirmed it perfectly by 1916. The photon was real.

de Broglie's Hypothesis — Matter as Waves

If light (thought to be a wave) can behave as particles, Louis de Broglie proposed in 1924 the converse: if the energy-momentum relation E = hf = pc holds for photons, perhaps all matter has an associated wavelength:

de Broglie Wavelengthλ = h/p = h/(mv) = ℏ/p     [for any particle of momentum p]

For a 100 eV electron (v ≈ 5.9 × 10⁶ m/s, m = 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg): λ = 6.63×10⁻³⁴/(9.11×10⁻³¹ × 5.9×10⁶) ≈ 1.23 × 10⁻¹⁰ m = 0.123 nm — precisely the spacing between atoms in crystals. This meant electrons should diffract from crystal planes just like X-rays.

The Double-Slit Experiment — The Heart of Quantum Mechanics

Richard Feynman called the double-slit experiment "the only mystery" of quantum mechanics — it contains all the strangeness of the quantum world in a single setup. The experiment proceeds as follows:

  1. Fire electrons (or photons, or atoms) one at a time through a barrier with two narrow slits.
  2. Detect each particle at a screen on the other side — each always arrives as a single localised point.
  3. Accumulate many such detections — an interference pattern of alternating bright and dark bands gradually emerges.
  4. When a detector is placed at the slits to determine which slit each electron passes through, the interference pattern disappears — replaced by two single-slit distributions.

The conclusions are inescapable and revolutionary: (1) Each electron, by itself, passes through both slits simultaneously — interfering with itself. (2) The electron has no definite trajectory — only a probability amplitude (wave function) that evolves through space. (3) The act of measurement (observing which slit) irrevocably disturbs the electron, destroying the interference. Knowledge of the path destroys the interference pattern.

Double-Slit Fringe PositionsBright fringes: y_m = mλL/d     Dark fringes: y_m = (m+½)λL/d
[L = slit-to-screen distance, d = slit separation]

Wave-Particle Duality — Complementarity

Niels Bohr formalised this apparent contradiction in his complementarity principle: wave-like and particle-like behaviour are complementary aspects of quantum objects. Experimental conditions that reveal wave behaviour (interference, diffraction) necessarily preclude the simultaneous determination of particle-like properties (definite trajectory, which-slit information), and vice versa. The two aspects complement rather than contradict each other — together they provide a complete description, but neither alone is sufficient.

The mathematical resolution is the wave function ψ(x,t): a complex-valued field whose squared modulus |ψ|² gives the probability density for finding the particle at position x at time t. The wave function obeys the superposition principle (waves add), producing interference. But measurement collapses the wave function to a single outcome (particle detected at a specific location).

🔬 Modern Applications of Wave-Particle Duality

Electron microscopes exploit de Broglie wavelengths (~0.001 nm for 100 kV electrons) to image individual atoms — resolution ~100× better than optical microscopes. The scanning tunnelling microscope uses electron tunnelling (a pure wave effect) to image atomic surfaces with sub-Ångström precision, achieving the first images of individual atoms. X-ray crystallography uses the wave nature of X-rays to determine protein structures — determining the structures of DNA, haemoglobin, and thousands of pharmaceuticals.
