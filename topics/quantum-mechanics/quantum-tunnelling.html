The Classical Impossibility

In classical mechanics, a particle approaching a potential energy barrier higher than its total energy is completely reflected — it cannot enter the classically forbidden region. The kinetic energy would become negative, which is physically meaningless classically. In quantum mechanics, this changes fundamentally: the wave function does not abruptly vanish at the barrier edge. It penetrates exponentially into the classically forbidden region, and if the barrier has finite thickness, the wave function emerges on the other side with non-zero amplitude. There is a finite probability that the particle is transmitted — it has "tunnelled" through the barrier.

The Square Barrier — Exact Solution

Consider a particle of energy E approaching a rectangular potential barrier of height V₀ and width a. For E < V₀ (tunnelling case), inside the barrier the Schrödinger equation gives exponentially decaying (evanescent) solutions:

Wave Function Inside Barrier (E < V₀)ψ(x) = A e^(κx) + B e^(−κx)     κ = √[2m(V₀−E)]/ℏ

Matching boundary conditions at both edges and computing probability currents gives the exact transmission coefficient:

Transmission Coefficient (Exact)T = [1 + V₀² sinh²(κa) / (4E(V₀−E))]⁻¹

For thick or tall barriers (κa ≫ 1): T ≈ 16(E/V₀)(1−E/V₀) e^(−2κa). The exponential e^(−2κa) dominates — tunnelling probability falls exponentially with barrier width a and with √(V₀−E). This exponential sensitivity is the key feature exploited in technological applications.

The WKB Approximation

For smoothly varying potentials V(x), the Wentzel-Kramers-Brillouin (WKB) semiclassical approximation gives the transmission probability:

WKB Tunnelling ProbabilityT ≈ exp[−2∫√(2m(V(x)−E))/ℏ dx]     (integral over classically forbidden region)

The WKB result applies whenever the potential varies slowly on the scale of the de Broglie wavelength. It correctly predicts the exponential dependence on barrier height, width, and particle mass. For the Coulomb barrier (V ∝ 1/r), the integral can be evaluated analytically — giving the Gamow factor used in nuclear physics.

Alpha Decay — Gamow's Theory

George Gamow (1928) explained radioactive alpha decay using quantum tunnelling — one of the first quantum mechanical applications to nuclear physics. An alpha particle (helium-4 nucleus) inside a heavy nucleus is trapped by the strong nuclear force, but faces a Coulomb repulsion barrier outside. The Gamow factor determines the tunnelling probability:

Gamow Factor (Alpha Decay)G = 2∫ κ(r) dr = (πZe²/ℏ)√(2M/E) − 4√(2MRV₀)/ℏ

The decay half-life t₁/₂ ∝ e^G. Because G depends exponentially on E (the alpha particle energy), tiny differences in energy produce enormous differences in half-life — the Geiger-Nuttall law: log(t₁/₂) ∝ 1/√E. Polonium-212 (E = 8.78 MeV) has t₁/₂ = 0.3 μs; Uranium-238 (E = 4.27 MeV) has t₁/₂ = 4.5 × 10⁹ years — a factor of 10²⁴ difference from a 2× change in energy, all explained by quantum tunnelling.

Scanning Tunnelling Microscope

The scanning tunnelling microscope (STM), invented by Binnig and Rohrer in 1981 (Nobel Prize 1986), directly exploits quantum tunnelling to image individual atoms. A sharp metallic tip is positioned within ~0.5–1 nm of a conducting surface. At this distance, electrons tunnel through the vacuum gap between tip and surface. The tunnelling current is exquisitely sensitive to gap distance d:

STM Tunnelling CurrentI ∝ V · e^(−2κd)     κ ≈ √(2mφ)/ℏ ≈ 10⁻¹⁰ m⁻¹ (for φ ~ few eV)

With κ ≈ 10¹⁰ m⁻¹, changing d by just 0.1 nm (1 Å) changes I by e^(2) ≈ 7.4×. This means a change of one atomic diameter in height changes the current by nearly an order of magnitude — providing sub-Ångström height resolution. STM was used to create the first images of individual silicon and carbon atoms, and has since been used to position individual atoms on surfaces (the famous IBM "quantum corral" of 48 iron atoms on copper, forming a ring that shows quantum interference patterns of trapped electrons).

💻 Quantum Computing: Josephson Junctions

Superconducting qubits — the basis of IBM, Google, and most quantum computers — exploit Cooper pair tunnelling through Josephson junctions (two superconductors separated by a thin insulator). The quantum states |0⟩ and |1⟩ correspond to different quantum states of the superconducting circuit, and quantum gates are implemented by microwave pulses. Google's Sycamore processor (2019) used 53 transmon qubits based on Josephson junctions to achieve "quantum advantage" — performing a specific computation in 200 seconds that would take a classical supercomputer ~10,000 years (though the classical estimate has been disputed).
