The Indispensable Approximation

Only a handful of quantum systems have exact analytic solutions: the free particle, particle in a box, harmonic oscillator, hydrogen atom, and a few others. Every real system of interest — multi-electron atoms, molecules, nuclei, solid-state systems, quantum field theories — requires approximation. Perturbation theory is the most widely used approximation in all of quantum physics. It applies when the Hamiltonian can be split as Ĥ = Ĥ₀ + λĤ', where Ĥ₀ has known solutions and λĤ' is "small" compared to level spacings of Ĥ₀.

Time-Independent Perturbation Theory

Expanding energies and states in powers of the coupling λ and collecting terms order by order:

First-Order Energy CorrectionE_n^(1) = ⟨n⁰|Ĥ'|n⁰⟩     (matrix element of Ĥ' in unperturbed eigenstate)
First-Order State Correction|n^(1)⟩ = Σ_{m≠n} [⟨m⁰|Ĥ'|n⁰⟩/(E_n^(0)−E_m^(0))] |m⁰⟩
Second-Order Energy CorrectionE_n^(2) = Σ_{m≠n} |⟨m⁰|Ĥ'|n⁰⟩|² / (E_n^(0) − E_m^(0))

The second-order correction is always negative for the ground state (all denominators E₀−E_m < 0 for m > 0), lowering the ground state energy. Physically: the perturbation mixes in excited states, allowing the system to "lower" its effective energy through virtual transitions.

Physical Examples of First-Order Perturbation

Zeeman Effect (weak field): An external magnetic field B adds Ĥ' = −μ⃗·B⃗ = μ_B(L̂_z + 2Ŝ_z)B. First-order correction: ΔE = μ_B B m_j (using good quantum numbers j, m_j with spin-orbit coupling). Each m_j sublevel shifts by a different amount, splitting spectral lines — the simulation above shows this splitting.

Spin-Orbit Coupling: In the electron's rest frame, the moving nucleus creates a magnetic field that interacts with the electron's spin magnetic moment: Ĥ_SO = ξ(r)L̂·Ŝ where ξ(r) = e²/(8πε₀m²c²r³). This perturbation breaks the l degeneracy, splitting each l>0 level into doublets at j = l±½. For hydrogen: ΔE_SO ~ α²E_n/n (α ≈ 1/137), explaining the fine structure of spectral lines.

Degenerate Perturbation Theory

When multiple unperturbed states share the same energy (degeneracy), the standard denominators E_n^(0)−E_m^(0) vanish and the expansion breaks down. The resolution: diagonalise the perturbation matrix Ĥ'_mn = ⟨m⁰|Ĥ'|n⁰⟩ within the degenerate subspace. The resulting eigenstates are the "good" quantum states that correctly describe the perturbed system to first order.

Physical example: Hydrogen's n=2 level has 4 degenerate states: 2s, 2p₀, 2p₊₁, 2p₋₁. Adding a uniform electric field E (Stark effect): Ĥ' = eEz. The matrix ⟨2s|eEz|2p₀⟩ = −3ea₀E (non-zero!); all other elements vanish by parity or selection rules. Diagonalising this 2×2 subspace: E±^(1) = ±3ea₀E — the linear Stark effect. Two levels split linearly in E, two remain unshifted.

Time-Dependent Perturbation Theory and Fermi's Golden Rule

When a periodic perturbation Ĥ'(t) = V·e^{−iωt} + V†·e^{iωt} is applied, transitions occur between energy eigenstates of Ĥ₀. The first-order transition rate from |i⟩ to |f⟩ is Fermi's Golden Rule:

Fermi's Golden Rule (transition rate)W_{i→f} = (2π/ℏ) |⟨f|V|i⟩|² ρ(E_f)     [transitions per second]

Where ρ(E_f) is the density of final states (available states per unit energy) at E_f = E_i ± ℏω. Despite being "first-order," Fermi's Golden Rule gives exact results when ρ(E_f) represents a true continuum. Applications include: atomic absorption cross-sections, nuclear beta decay rates, electron scattering in semiconductors, phonon scattering limiting electrical conductivity, and radioactive decay lifetimes.

🌙 The Lamb Shift

Standard perturbation theory predicts that hydrogen's 2s₁/₂ and 2p₁/₂ states are exactly degenerate (same n=2, same j=½). Willis Lamb measured in 1947 using microwave spectroscopy that they differ by 1057.8 MHz — the Lamb shift. This arises from the electron's interaction with vacuum fluctuations of the electromagnetic field (self-energy correction at one-loop in QED). Explaining the Lamb shift required the development of renormalised quantum electrodynamics. Lamb received the Nobel Prize in Physics 1955. The theoretical QED calculation of the Lamb shift now agrees with measurement to 12 significant figures — the most precisely tested prediction in physics.
