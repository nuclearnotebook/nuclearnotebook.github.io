From Philosophy to Experiment

For 30 years after the EPR paper (1935), the debate between Einstein and Bohr about quantum completeness and hidden variables seemed purely philosophical — a matter of interpretive preference with no experimental consequences. John Bell changed this in 1964 with one of the most important theorems in physics: any local hidden variable theory makes quantitatively different predictions from quantum mechanics, and these differences can be measured experimentally. Bell transformed a metaphysical debate into an experimental question.

Local Hidden Variable Theories

A local hidden variable (LHV) theory assumes: (1) Realism — measurement outcomes are predetermined by hidden variables λ carried by the particles, not created by measurement; (2) Locality — Alice's measurement outcome cannot be influenced by Bob's distant measurement or by his choice of measurement setting (no faster-than-light influences). Einstein believed both. Bell proved that these two seemingly reasonable assumptions lead to specific, testable constraints on correlations.

Derivation of the CHSH Inequality

For any LHV model, Alice gets result A(a,λ) = ±1 and Bob gets B(b,λ) = ±1 (where a, b are their measurement settings and λ is the hidden variable). The correlation for settings (a,b):

LHV CorrelationE(a,b) = ∫ A(a,λ)B(b,λ) ρ(λ) dλ

Since each A = ±1 and B = ±1, we can show that for any four settings a, a', b, b':

|A(a)[B(b)−B(b')] + A(a')[B(b)+B(b')]| ≤ 2 for each λ (since B(b)±B(b') = 0 or ±2)

Averaging over λ: S = |E(a,b) − E(a,b') + E(a',b) + E(a',b')| ≤ 2

Quantum Mechanical Violation

For the singlet state |ψ⁻⟩ = (|↑↓⟩−|↓↑⟩)/√2, quantum mechanics predicts:

QM Correlation (singlet state)E_QM(a,b) = −cos(θ_{ab})     (θ_{ab} = angle between measurement axes)

With optimal angles (a=0°, a'=45°, b=22.5°, b'=67.5°):

S_QM = |E(0°,22.5°) − E(0°,67.5°) + E(45°,22.5°) + E(45°,67.5°)| = |−cos22.5° + cos67.5° − cos22.5° − cos22.5°| = 2√2 ≈ 2.828

This violates the classical bound S ≤ 2 by a factor of √2. The Tsirelson bound (2√2) is the maximum any quantum theory can achieve — proven mathematically from the structure of quantum mechanics. Experiments consistently find S ≈ 2.4–2.7.

What Bell's Theorem Proves

Any theory that reproduces quantum mechanical predictions must violate at least one of the following: (1) Locality — no faster-than-light influence; or (2) Realism — measurement outcomes are predetermined. Einstein's programme of local realism is experimentally excluded. Nature is non-local in the quantum mechanical sense: measurement correlations between distant entangled particles cannot be explained by any local model. This does not enable faster-than-light signalling (the correlations are random — only after classical communication can the violation be established), but it proves that quantum non-locality is a real feature of nature.

🏆 Nobel Prize in Physics 2022

Alain Aspect, John Clauser, and Anton Zeilinger received the 2022 Nobel Prize in Physics "for experiments with entangled photons, establishing the violation of Bell inequalities and pioneering quantum information science." The Nobel Committee described their work as settling "an almost 90-year debate about the foundations of quantum mechanics" and proving "the violation of Bell inequalities means that quantum mechanics cannot be replaced by a theory that uses hidden variables." Beyond foundational physics, their techniques launched quantum cryptography, quantum teleportation, quantum computing, and quantum networks — technologies attracting >$35 billion in global investment annually by 2024.
