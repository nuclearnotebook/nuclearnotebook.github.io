The Superposition Principle

Quantum superposition is one of the most profound departures from classical physics. The superposition principle states that any linear combination of valid quantum states is itself a valid quantum state — a direct consequence of the linearity of the Schrödinger equation. A qubit (quantum bit) exists as |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ simultaneously in both states, not as either one with unknown probability.

General Superposition State|ψ⟩ = Σₙ cₙ|n⟩     with Σₙ |cₙ|² = 1 (normalisation)

The complex coefficients cₙ are probability amplitudes. The probability of measuring eigenvalue aₙ is P(aₙ) = |cₙ|² = |⟨n|ψ⟩|². Critically, before measurement, the system genuinely occupies all states simultaneously — not merely with unknown definite state. This is proven by interference experiments where quantum coherence between terms is physically observable.

Superposition vs. Classical Probability — The Key Distinction

Consider the state |+⟩ = (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2. Classically, you might interpret this as "the system is in state 0 or state 1 with equal probability — we just don't know which." But quantum superposition is fundamentally different. If you measure the observable whose eigenstates are |+⟩ and |−⟩ = (|0⟩ − |1⟩)/√2, the state |+⟩ gives a definite outcome (+1) every time. A classically mixed state would give random outcomes. The quantum coherence between |0⟩ and |1⟩ is real, measurable, and produces interference effects with no classical counterpart.

💡 The Bloch Sphere

Every pure qubit state can be represented as a point on the unit Bloch sphere: |ψ⟩ = cos(θ/2)|0⟩ + e^(iφ) sin(θ/2)|1⟩. North pole = |0⟩, south pole = |1⟩, equator = equal superpositions. The Bloch sphere provides geometric intuition for all single-qubit operations. Quantum gates are rotations of the Bloch vector. Schrödinger's Cat — Superposition at Macroscopic Scale

In 1935, Schrödinger proposed his famous thought experiment to highlight the absurdity of applying quantum superposition to macroscopic systems. A cat in a sealed box is coupled to a radioactive atom through a detector and poison vial. After one half-life, the joint state is:

Cat Entangled State|ψ⟩ = (1/√2)(|alive atom⟩|alive cat⟩ + |decayed atom⟩|dead cat⟩)

Schrödinger intended this as a reductio ad absurdum — cats cannot be simultaneously alive and dead. The resolution comes from decoherence: a cat interacts with ∼10²³ environmental degrees of freedom every nanosecond. Quantum coherence between the macroscopic "alive" and "dead" branches decays on timescales τ_D ∼ 10⁻²³ seconds — effectively instantaneous. We never observe macroscopic superpositions because the environment continuously "measures" the system, collapsing superpositions before they can be observed.

Quantum Computing — Exponential Superposition

The computational power of quantum computers arises directly from superposition. A classical n-bit register holds exactly one of 2ⁿ possible bit strings. An n-qubit register in superposition represents all 2ⁿ states simultaneously:

n-Qubit Superposition|ψ⟩ = Σ_{x=0}^{2ⁿ-1} cₓ|x⟩     (2ⁿ amplitudes simultaneously)

With just 300 qubits, more basis states are simultaneously represented than atoms in the observable universe (2³⁰⁰ ≈ 10⁹⁰). Quantum algorithms manipulate these amplitudes coherently to amplify the probability of correct answers. Shor's algorithm (1994) factors integers in polynomial time using superposition + quantum Fourier transform — exponentially faster than any known classical algorithm, threatening RSA encryption.

Coherence Time and Decoherence

Superposition is fragile. Any interaction with the environment entangles the quantum system with environmental degrees of freedom, causing the off-diagonal elements of the density matrix (the quantum coherences) to decay exponentially. The coherence time τ_c determines how long useful superposition can be maintained:

⚛ Quantum Zeno Effect

If a quantum system in superposition is measured repeatedly and rapidly, its evolution freezes — the "quantum Zeno effect." Each measurement projects the state back toward its initial configuration, preventing transitions. Confirmed experimentally with trapped ions (Itano et al., 1989): atoms in an unstable state, bombarded with measuring laser pulses at frequency f, show decay rate suppressed by factor ~(Γ/f)² compared to unmeasured rate Γ. Used in quantum error correction to stabilise qubits against transitions to error states.
