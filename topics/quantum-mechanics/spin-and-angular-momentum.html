The Discovery of Spin

In 1922, Otto Stern and Walther Gerlach passed a beam of silver atoms through a non-uniform magnetic field. Classical physics predicted the atoms would be deflected into a continuous smear (the magnetic moment could point in any direction). Instead, they observed exactly two spots on the detector — two discrete deflection values. This was the first direct measurement of quantised angular momentum, and specifically of a property that has no classical analogue: intrinsic spin.

Spin cannot be visualised as physical rotation — an electron has no measurable spatial extent, and the spin angular momentum ℏ/2 cannot be produced by any classical spinning motion consistent with special relativity. Spin is a purely quantum mechanical, relativistic property. Paul Dirac showed in 1928 that spin-½ emerges automatically from combining quantum mechanics with special relativity — it is not an additional assumption but a mathematical consequence.

Spin-½ Operators and Pauli Matrices

For a spin-½ particle, the spin operator is Ŝ = (ℏ/2)σ⃗, where σ⃗ are the Pauli matrices:

Pauli Matrices (2×2)σ_x = [[0,1],[1,0]]    σ_y = [[0,−i],[i,0]]    σ_z = [[1,0],[0,−1]]

The Pauli matrices satisfy σᵢ² = I (identity), σ_xσ_y = iσ_z (cyclic), and anticommutation {σᵢ,σⱼ} = 2δᵢⱼ. The spin eigenstates are: |↑⟩ = [1,0] (eigenvalue +ℏ/2 for Ŝ_z) and |↓⟩ = [0,1] (eigenvalue −ℏ/2). Any spin state can be written: |ψ⟩ = α|↑⟩ + β|↓⟩ — a qubit, the basic unit of quantum computing.

Spin EigenstatesŜ_z|↑⟩ = +(ℏ/2)|↑⟩     Ŝ_z|↓⟩ = −(ℏ/2)|↓⟩

The Stern-Gerlach Experiment in Detail

A magnetic dipole in a non-uniform field experiences force F_z = μ_z (∂B_z/∂z). For an electron: μ_z = −gₑ(e/2mₑ)Ŝ_z = ∓gₑμ_B/2. The g-factor gₑ ≈ 2 means the force is F_z = ∓gₑμ_B(∂B_z/∂z)/2, deflecting atoms to two discrete positions. The experiment beautifully demonstrates key quantum principles:

  • Angular momentum is quantised in units of ℏ/2
  • Measurement is irreversible: measuring S_z places the atom in a definite spin eigenstate
  • Measuring a different component (S_x) after measuring S_z gives random results — S_x measurement has "erased" the S_z information
  • Two consecutive S_z measurements always agree — eigenstate collapses are stable

Spinors — The Strange Transformation of Spin-½

Spin-½ states (spinors) have a remarkable and counterintuitive transformation property: a rotation by 2π (360°) about any axis transforms |ψ⟩ → −|ψ⟩. The spinor requires a 4π (720°) rotation to return to its original state. This behaviour — with no classical analogue — was confirmed experimentally by neutron interferometry (Rauch et al., 1975; Werner et al., 1975). A neutron beam split and sent through a magnetic field that rotates its spin by 2π produces an interference pattern shifted by π (destructive interference) relative to the unrotated beam — direct observation of the spinor's 4π periodicity.

Addition of Angular Momenta

When combining angular momenta j₁ and j₂, the total J can take values |j₁−j₂| ≤ J ≤ j₁+j₂ in integer steps. For two spin-½ electrons:

Combining Two Spin-½ ParticlesJ = 0 (singlet): |S⟩ = (|↑↓⟩ − |↓↑⟩)/√2   (antisymmetric)
J = 1 (triplet): |T,+1⟩ = |↑↑⟩, |T,0⟩ = (|↑↓⟩+|↓↑⟩)/√2, |T,-1⟩ = |↓↓⟩

The singlet state is the maximally entangled Bell state used in quantum cryptography and Bell tests. The Clebsch-Gordan coefficients ⟨j₁m₁j₂m₂|JM⟩ give the decomposition between product states and total angular momentum states — essential for atomic spectroscopy selection rules, nuclear level coupling schemes, and the classification of composite particles in particle physics.

🧠 Spin in Quantum Computing

The electron spin is a natural qubit: |↑⟩ = |0⟩, |↓⟩ = |1⟩. Quantum computers based on spin qubits include: silicon spin qubits (Intel, UNSW) exploiting electron spins in gate-defined quantum dots; trapped ion qubits (IonQ, Quantinuum) using nuclear spins of ions; nitrogen-vacancy (NV) centre qubits in diamond using the electronic spin of the NV defect. Spin qubits in silicon have the advantage of compatibility with existing CMOS fabrication — potentially enabling millions of qubits on a single chip using conventional semiconductor manufacturing.
