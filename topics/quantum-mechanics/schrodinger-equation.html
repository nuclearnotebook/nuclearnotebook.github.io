The Birth of Wave Mechanics

By 1925, physicists knew that particles had wave properties (de Broglie, 1924) and that energy was quantised (Planck, 1900; Bohr, 1913). What was missing was a proper wave equation — an equation of motion for the quantum wave function analogous to Maxwell's equations for electromagnetic waves or Newton's equation for particles. Erwin Schrödinger, motivated by de Broglie's work, derived his equation in early 1926 using an analogy with classical wave optics. The equation he found, now bearing his name, is one of the most important differential equations in all of science.

The Time-Dependent Schrödinger Equation

The full Schrödinger equation describes the time evolution of the quantum state ψ(r,t) — the wave function. In 3D:

Time-Dependent Schrödinger Equation (TDSE)iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = Ĥψ = [−ℏ²/(2m) ∇² + V(r,t)] ψ

Where: ℏ = h/(2π) = 1.055 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s (reduced Planck constant), m is particle mass, ∇² is the Laplacian operator (∂²/∂x² + ∂²/∂y² + ∂²/∂z²), V(r,t) is the potential energy, and Ĥ is the Hamiltonian operator (total energy). The equation is first-order in time and second-order in space — a linear partial differential equation.

The imaginary unit i on the left side is not a technicality — it is fundamental. It gives ψ a complex phase that rotates in time, allowing interference between different states. A purely real wave equation (like the classical wave equation) could not produce the destructive interference needed to explain, for example, the dark fringes in the double-slit experiment.

Physical Interpretation — The Born Rule

What does ψ(r,t) mean physically? Schrödinger initially thought ψ represented a real charge density — but this interpretation quickly failed (it predicted atoms would radiate continuously and collapse). Max Born proposed the correct interpretation in 1926:

Born Rule — Probability DensityP(r,t) = |ψ(r,t)|² = ψ*(r,t)·ψ(r,t)

|ψ(r,t)|² dV is the probability of finding the particle in volume element dV at position r at time t. The wave function itself is not directly observable (it is complex-valued), but its modulus squared is a real, non-negative, observable probability density. This probabilistic interpretation — shocking to many physicists including Einstein and Schrödinger himself — is now the foundational postulate of quantum mechanics.

The normalisation condition ∫|ψ|² dV = 1 (over all space) ensures that the particle must be found somewhere — total probability = 1.

The Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation (TISE)

When the potential V is time-independent (the most common case in atomic and molecular physics), we can separate variables: ψ(r,t) = φ(r)·e^(−iEt/ℏ). The spatial part φ(r) satisfies the time-independent Schrödinger equation:

Time-Independent Schrödinger EquationĤφ = Eφ     [an eigenvalue equation: Ĥ is operator, E is eigenvalue, φ is eigenstate]

This is an eigenvalue problem — we seek the values of E (energy eigenvalues) and corresponding φ (energy eigenstates or stationary states) for which the equation holds. The full time-dependent solution is then the superposition of all stationary states:

General Solution (TDSE)ψ(r,t) = Σn c_n φ_n(r) e^(−iE_n t/ℏ)

Particle in an Infinite Square Well

The simplest non-trivial system: a particle confined to 0 ≤ x ≤ L with infinite potential walls. The TISE gives:

Particle in a Box — Solutionsφ_n(x) = √(2/L) sin(nπx/L)     E_n = n²π²ℏ²/(2mL²)     n = 1, 2, 3, ...

Key results: (1) Energy is quantised — only discrete values E_n allowed. (2) Ground state (n=1) has non-zero energy E₁ = π²ℏ²/(2mL²) — the zero-point energy, a quantum effect with no classical counterpart. (3) Wave functions are standing waves with n half-wavelengths fitting in the box. (4) As L → ∞ (free particle), energy levels become continuous — recovering classical behaviour.

Properties of the Schrödinger Equation

  • Linearity: If ψ₁ and ψ₂ are solutions, so is any linear combination c₁ψ₁ + c₂ψ₂ — enabling superposition.
  • Conservation of probability: ∂|ψ|²/∂t + ∇·j = 0 (continuity equation), where j = (ℏ/2mi)(ψ*∇ψ − ψ∇ψ*) is the probability current. Total probability is conserved.
  • Determinism in ψ: Given ψ at t=0, the TDSE uniquely determines ψ at all future times. The evolution of ψ is perfectly deterministic. Randomness enters only upon measurement (Born rule).
  • Real energy eigenvalues: Because Ĥ is Hermitian, all eigenvalues E are real — physically meaningful measurable energies.
🏆 Immediate Triumphs of the Schrödinger Equation

Within months of publication, Schrödinger applied his equation to the hydrogen atom and derived the exact energy levels E_n = −13.6/n² eV — reproducing Bohr's empirical formula from first principles, and additionally predicting the correct angular structure of atomic orbitals. Heisenberg's matrix mechanics (1925) was simultaneously developed and proved mathematically equivalent. The two formalisms — wave mechanics and matrix mechanics — are unified in Dirac's bra-ket formalism, the modern language of quantum mechanics.
