Feynman's Sum Over Histories, Stationary Phase, and Quantum Field Theory

A Third Way to Do Quantum Mechanics

By 1948, quantum mechanics had been formulated in two mathematically equivalent ways: Schrödinger's wave mechanics (differential equations for ψ(x,t)) and Heisenberg's matrix mechanics (operator algebra). Richard Feynman, working from a cryptic 1933 paper by Dirac, developed a third formulation — the path integral — that is physically the most intuitive and mathematically the most powerful for quantum field theory and statistical mechanics.

Feynman's starting point: in quantum mechanics, the amplitude ⟨x_f,t_f|x_i,t_i⟩ for a particle to travel from (x_i,t_i) to (x_f,t_f) is a complex number. Feynman asked: how does this amplitude arise? His answer: it is the coherent sum of contributions from all possible paths connecting the two points, each path contributing phase e^{iS[path]/ℏ} where S is the classical action of that path.

The Path Integral Formula

Feynman Path Integral (Propagator)K(x_f,t_f; x_i,t_i) = ∫𝒟[x(t)] · exp(iS[x(t)]/ℏ)

The integral ∫𝒟[x(t)] is a functional integral — a sum over all paths x(t) satisfying x(t_i) = x_i and x(t_f) = x_f, not just the classical one. The action S[x] = ∫_{t_i}^{t_f} L(x,ẋ,t) dt is the classical action functional. Each path contributes with equal amplitude but different phase e^{iS/ℏ}.

How the Classical Limit Emerges

The stationary phase approximation explains why quantum mechanics reduces to classical mechanics for large objects. For paths near the classical trajectory x_cl(t) (where δS = 0 by the principle of least action), the phase S/ℏ varies slowly — contributions add coherently. For paths far from x_cl(t), the phase oscillates rapidly as the path varies, and contributions cancel by destructive interference.

Quantitatively: the "width" of the coherence band around the classical path is Δx ~ √(ℏτ/m), where τ is the time interval. For an electron (m ~ 10⁻³⁰ kg, τ ~ 1 ns): Δx ~ 10⁻¹¹ m ~ atomic scale — quantum fluctuations matter. For a golf ball (m ~ 0.05 kg, τ ~ 1 s): Δx ~ 10⁻¹⁶ m — far smaller than any relevant physical scale. Classical mechanics is exact for all practical purposes.

Connection to Classical Mechanics

The path integral reveals that Feynman's formulation and Hamilton's principle of least action are dual descriptions of the same physics. Hamilton's principle (classical mechanics): the physical path is the one that makes S stationary, δS = 0. Feynman's path integral (quantum mechanics): all paths contribute; the path that makes S stationary is the one where contributions add most coherently. Classical mechanics is the quantum mechanics of zero interference — the saddle-point approximation of the path integral.

Path Integrals in Quantum Field Theory

The path integral's greatest power is in quantum field theory, where it extends naturally to sum over field configurations φ(x,t):

QFT Generating FunctionalZ[J] = ∫𝒟[φ] exp(i∫[ℒ(φ,∂_μφ) + J(x)φ(x)] d⁴x / ℏ)

All correlation functions, scattering amplitudes, and Green's functions of the quantum field theory follow from derivatives of Z[J] with respect to the source J(x). Perturbation theory in the coupling constant λ corresponds to expanding e^{iS_int/ℏ} and computing Gaussian integrals — producing the Feynman diagram expansion automatically.

Euclidean Path Integral and Statistical Mechanics

Analytic continuation to imaginary time τ = it (Wick rotation) transforms the quantum path integral into the partition function of statistical mechanics:

Euclidean Path Integral = Partition FunctionZ_E = ∫𝒟[φ] exp(−S_E[φ]/ℏ) = Tr(e^{−βĤ})

This profound connection — quantum field theory at imaginary time equals statistical mechanics at inverse temperature β = 1/(k_BT) — enables lattice QCD (computing hadron masses from first principles), quantum Monte Carlo simulations, and the renormalisation group treatment of critical phenomena in phase transitions.

🧮 Lattice QCD — Proton Mass from First Principles

Lattice QCD discretises spacetime onto a 4D grid and evaluates the path integral Z = ∫𝒟[A]𝒟[ψ]e^{−S_QCD} numerically using Monte Carlo sampling. Computing the proton mass requires evaluating Grassmann (fermionic) path integrals over quark fields ψ and bosonic path integrals over gluon fields A. The 2010 calculation (Budapest-Marseille-Wuppertal collaboration) computed the proton-to-electron mass ratio m_p/m_e = 1836 from QCD parameters alone, agreeing with experiment to 1.5%. This required ~100 million CPU-core hours on leadership supercomputers — the most ambitious first-principles calculation in physics history.
