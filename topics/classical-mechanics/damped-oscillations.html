Beyond Ideal Oscillators — The Reality of Damping

The idealised SHM of the previous topic — perpetual oscillation at constant amplitude — never occurs in nature. All real oscillators lose energy to their environment: springs have internal friction, pendulums encounter air resistance, electrical oscillators have resistance. This energy loss is damping. Far from being merely a correction, damping profoundly changes oscillator behaviour and is deliberately engineered into systems for control and stability. The interplay of damping, natural frequency, and driving forces produces resonance — one of the most important and spectacular phenomena in all of physics.

The Damped Oscillator Equation

For viscous (velocity-proportional) damping, the equation of motion for a mass-spring-dashpot system is:

Damped Oscillator ODEm(d²x/dt²) + b(dx/dt) + kx = 0     [b = damping coefficient, N·s/m]

Dividing by m and writing γ = b/(2m) (damping rate) and ω₀ = √(k/m) (natural angular frequency):

Standard Formd²x/dt² + 2γ(dx/dt) + ω₀²x = 0

The characteristic equation is λ² + 2γλ + ω₀² = 0, with roots λ = −γ ± √(γ²−ω₀²). The three qualitatively distinct regimes depend on the relative magnitudes of γ and ω₀:

Three Damping Regimes

ConditionNameSolutionPhysical Behaviour
γ < ω₀Underdampedx = Ae^(−γt) cos(ω_d t + φ), ω_d = √(ω₀²−γ²)Oscillates with exponentially decreasing amplitude
γ = ω₀Critically dampedx = (A + Bt)e^(−γt)Returns to equilibrium in minimum time, no oscillation
γ > ω₀Overdampedx = e^(−γt)[Ae^(λ₁t) + Be^(λ₂t)]Returns to equilibrium slowly, no oscillation

The damped frequency ω_d < ω₀: damping slows the oscillation. For light damping (γ ≪ ω₀), ω_d ≈ ω₀ — the frequency change is negligible. The amplitude decays as e^(−γt) — reducing to 1/e of initial amplitude in time τ = 1/γ.

Quality Factor Q

Quality FactorQ = ω₀/(2γ) = ω₀m/b     [dimensionless]

The Q-factor is the single most important parameter characterising an oscillator's damping. It can be interpreted several ways:

  • Q = π × (number of oscillations for amplitude to fall by factor e^π ≈ 23)
  • Q = (energy stored) / (energy lost per radian of oscillation) × 2π
  • Q = (resonant frequency) / (bandwidth) — the sharpness of the resonance peak
SystemQ valueImplication
Car suspension~0.5 (critically damped)Returns to level instantly after bump, no oscillation
Guitar string~100–1000Rings for many seconds

Quartz crystal oscillator~10⁴–10⁶Very precise frequency — basis of quartz clocks

Forced Oscillations and Resonance

When a damped oscillator is driven by a periodic force F(t) = F₀ cos(ωt):

Steady-State AmplitudeA(ω) = F₀/m / √((ω₀²−ω²)² + (2γω)²)

Amplitude peaks near ω = ω₀ (resonance). For light damping (Q ≫ 1), resonance amplitude: A_res ≈ F₀/(2mγω₀) = QF₀/(mω₀²) — Q times the static displacement F₀/(mω₀²) = F₀/k. High Q → enormous resonance amplification.

⚠ Resonance Disasters

Tacoma Narrows Bridge (1940): torsional resonance at 0.2 Hz driven by aeroelastic flutter collapsed the bridge 4 months after opening. Angers Bridge (1850): resonance from soldiers marching in step. Millennium Bridge London (2000): lateral resonance at 1 Hz driven by pedestrians synchronising to the bridge's sway — fixed by adding 37 tuned viscous dampers. These disasters established that resonance analysis is mandatory for all bridge designs, and that buildings must be designed to have natural frequencies well away from earthquake and wind forcing frequencies.
