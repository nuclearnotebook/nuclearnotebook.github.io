From Observation to Law: Kepler's Achievement

Johannes Kepler spent years analysing Tycho Brahe's meticulous naked-eye observations of planetary positions — the finest pre-telescopic astronomical data ever assembled. Between 1609 and 1619, Kepler extracted three precise mathematical laws of planetary motion from this data. He had no theoretical explanation — the laws were purely empirical. Newton later showed they follow exactly as consequences of his law of universal gravitation, providing the theoretical foundation Kepler lacked.

Kepler's First Law — Law of Ellipses

Each planet moves in an ellipse with the Sun at one focus.

An ellipse is characterised by its semi-major axis a (half the longest diameter), semi-minor axis b, and eccentricity e = √(1−b²/a²). The Sun sits at one focus, located at distance ae from the centre. Key orbital positions:

Kepler's Second Law — Law of Equal Areas

A line from the Sun to the planet sweeps equal areas in equal times.

This is a direct consequence of conservation of angular momentum. Since gravity acts along the Sun-planet line (no tangential force component), the torque about the Sun is zero, and L = mvr = constant. When r decreases (planet closer to Sun), v must increase to maintain L. Equal areas in equal times follows mathematically from L = const.

Angular Momentum — Kepler's 2nd LawL = m·v·r·sinφ = constant     → vp·rp = va·ra

At perihelion and aphelion, velocity is perpendicular to the radius vector (φ = 90°), so the simplified relation vprp = vara holds. Earth moves at 30.3 km/s at perihelion (January) and 29.3 km/s at aphelion (July) — a 3.4% variation.

Kepler's Third Law — Law of Periods

Kepler's Third LawT² = (4π²/GM) a³     [T in seconds, a in metres, M = mass of central body]

The square of orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis. The proportionality constant 4π²/GM depends only on the central mass M — not on the orbiting body's mass. This universality means Kepler's Third Law can be used to measure the mass of any celestial body with orbiting companions: M = 4π²a³/(GT²).

The Vis-viva Equation

The vis-viva equation (from energy conservation) gives orbital speed at any distance r for an orbit with semi-major axis a:

Vis-viva Equationv² = GM(2/r − 1/a)

Special cases: circular orbit (r = a): vcirc = √(GM/r); parabolic escape (a → ∞): vesc = √(2GM/r) = √2 · vcirc.

Orbital Energy and Manoeuvres

Total Orbital EnergyE = KE + PE = −GMm/(2a)

The total energy depends only on the semi-major axis — not on eccentricity. To raise an orbit (go from a lower to a higher orbit), you must increase energy (do positive work, fire engines in the direction of motion). The Hohmann transfer orbit is the most fuel-efficient two-burn manoeuvre between circular orbits: one burn at the lower orbit raises aphelion to the higher orbit, a second burn at aphelion circularises the orbit.

🛰 GPS Satellite Orbits

GPS satellites orbit at a = 26,560 km (altitude ~20,200 km). Using Kepler's Third Law: T = 2π√(a³/GME) = 2π√((26.56×10⁶)³/(3.986×10¹⁴)) = 43,082 s ≈ 11 hours 58 minutes. With 24 active satellites in 6 orbital planes, at least 4 satellites are always visible from any point on Earth, enabling 3D position fixes to within 3–5 metres (civilian) or ~0.3 m (military/differential GPS).
