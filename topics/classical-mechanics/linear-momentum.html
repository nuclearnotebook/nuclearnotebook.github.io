Momentum — The Fundamental Quantity of Motion

Linear momentum is one of the most fundamental conserved quantities in physics. Defined as the product of mass and velocity, momentum is a vector quantity that encodes both the "amount" and direction of motion. The conservation of linear momentum — that the total momentum of an isolated system is unchanged — follows directly from Newton's Third Law and is one of the most powerful principles in all of mechanics.

Linear Momentump⃗ = m·v⃗     [kg·m/s]

Newton's Second Law is more naturally expressed in terms of momentum. Newton himself wrote F = dp/dt, not F = ma. The more general form remains valid even when mass changes (rocket propulsion) or when relativistic effects are important (where p = γmv with γ the Lorentz factor):

Newton's 2nd Law — General FormF⃗net = dp⃗/dt

Impulse and the Impulse-Momentum Theorem

The impulse J⃗ of a force is the integral of force over the time it acts — the "area under the F-t curve":

ImpulseJ⃗ = ∫F⃗ dt = Δp⃗ = m·v⃗f − m·v⃗i

The Impulse-Momentum Theorem is the statement that impulse equals the change in momentum. It is enormously useful in collision problems where the force varies during impact but the duration Δt is known. The average force is Favg = Δp/Δt. For a given momentum change Δp:

  • Short collision time Δt → Very large average force (hard surface: car hitting concrete wall)
  • Long collision time Δt → Smaller average force (crumple zones, airbags, padding: same Δp spread over longer time)

This is the physics behind automotive safety: crumple zones increase the collision time from ~50 ms to ~150 ms — tripling the collision time, reducing average force by one-third, reducing injury severity dramatically.

Conservation of Linear Momentum

For an isolated system (no net external force), the total momentum is conserved — at all times, for all internal interactions, however violent:

Conservation LawΣp⃗initial = Σp⃗final     (no external forces)

This follows from Newton's Third Law: every internal force F⃗A on B is matched by −F⃗B on A. Their contributions to dp⃗total/dt cancel exactly, leaving the total momentum unchanged. Explosions, collisions, fragmentation — the total momentum vector is conserved regardless.

Elastic Collision Equations — 1D

For a 1D elastic collision between m₁ (initial velocity u₁) and m₂ (initial velocity u₂), solving the simultaneous conservation equations for momentum and KE:

Final Velocities — 1D Elastic Collisionv₁ = [(m₁−m₂)u₁ + 2m₂u₂] / (m₁+m₂)
v₂ = [(m₂−m₁)u₂ + 2m₁u₁] / (m₁+m₂)

Special cases illuminate the physics:

  • Equal masses (m₁ = m₂): v₁ = u₂, v₂ = u₁ — velocities are simply exchanged. This explains Newton's cradle exactly.
  • Very heavy hits very light (m₁ ≫ m₂): v₁ ≈ u₁ (heavy barely slows), v₂ ≈ 2u₁ − u₂ (light bounces off at ~twice the heavy's speed). A bowling ball barely slows when hitting a ping-pong ball.
  • Very light hits very heavy at rest (m₁ ≪ m₂, u₂=0): v₁ ≈ −u₁ (light bounces back), v₂ ≈ 0 (heavy barely moves). A ping-pong ball bouncing off a concrete wall.

Perfectly Inelastic Collision

Combined Velocity After Perfectly Inelastic Collisionvf = (m₁u₁ + m₂u₂) / (m₁ + m₂)

The KE lost = ½m₁u₁² + ½m₂u₂² − ½(m₁+m₂)vf². This is always positive (≥ 0), confirming that KE can only be lost, never gained, in an inelastic collision. Maximum KE loss occurs when the objects stick together (perfectly inelastic). In the CM frame, all kinetic energy is lost in a perfectly inelastic collision.

Rocket Propulsion and Variable Mass

A rocket accelerates by expelling mass (exhaust) at high velocity. The Tsiolkovsky rocket equation follows from momentum conservation for a variable-mass system:

Tsiolkovsky Rocket EquationΔv = ve · ln(m₀/mf)     [ve = exhaust velocity]

Where m₀ is initial mass and mf is final (dry) mass. The logarithmic dependence means diminishing returns: to double Δv, you must square the mass ratio. The Saturn V moon rocket started at 2,970 tonnes and arrived in orbit with ~130 tonnes — a mass ratio of ~22.8, giving Δv ≈ ve·ln(22.8) ≈ 9.7 km/s with ve ≈ 3.1 km/s.

🚀 Two-Stage Rockets

Staging (discarding empty fuel tanks) dramatically improves performance by reducing mf. A two-stage rocket achieves Δv = ve·ln(m₀₁/mf1) + ve·ln(m₀₂/mf2), which for typical staging ratios gives ~40% more Δv than a single-stage vehicle with the same total mass. This is why no single-stage rocket has ever achieved orbital velocity.
