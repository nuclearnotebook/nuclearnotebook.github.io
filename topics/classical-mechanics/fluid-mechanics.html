Why Fluid Mechanics Matters

Fluid mechanics governs the flow of blood through arteries, the lift that keeps aircraft aloft, the dynamics of weather and ocean currents, the efficiency of turbines and pumps, and the flow of natural gas through pipelines. It is arguably the branch of classical mechanics with the widest range of practical applications. The governing equations — the Navier-Stokes equations — are so mathematically complex that their solution in turbulent regimes remains one of the Clay Mathematics Millennium Prize Problems (unsolved as of 2024).

Hydrostatics — Fluids at Rest

Pressure at Depth h in Static FluidP(h) = P₀ + ρgh     [P₀ = surface pressure, ρ = fluid density]

Pressure in a static fluid increases linearly with depth, acts equally in all directions (Pascal's principle), and depends only on depth — not on the shape of the container. Consequences: a hydraulic jack (Pascal's machine) transmits pressure throughout a fluid to multiply force: F₂ = F₁(A₂/A₁). A small force on a small piston creates a large force on a large piston, with the same pressure in the fluid.

Archimedes' Principle — Buoyancy

Buoyant ForceF_b = ρ_fluid · V_displaced · g     (upward)

Any object partially or fully submerged in a fluid experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of fluid displaced. Origin: the fluid pressure on the bottom face of a submerged object is greater than on the top face (pressure increases with depth), creating a net upward force. An object floats if F_b ≥ mg — i.e., if its average density ≤ fluid density. Ships float despite being made of steel because their average density (including the hollow hull filled with air and cargo) is less than seawater.

Continuity Equation — Conservation of Mass in Flow

Continuity Equation (incompressible flow)A₁v₁ = A₂v₂ = Q = constant     [Q = volume flow rate, m³/s]

For an incompressible fluid (ρ = const) in a pipe, the volume flow rate Q is constant along the pipe. Where the cross-sectional area decreases, velocity increases — and vice versa. This is why rivers narrow at rapids and move faster, and blood accelerates through stenosed (narrowed) arteries.

Bernoulli's Equation

Bernoulli's Principle (ideal fluid, along streamline)P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant

Bernoulli's equation is energy conservation per unit volume for ideal (inviscid, incompressible, steady, irrotational) flow. The three terms are pressure energy (P), kinetic energy density (½ρv²), and gravitational potential energy density (ρgh). Where velocity is high, pressure is low. Applications:

  • Aerofoil lift: Air accelerates over the curved upper surface → lower pressure above wing → net upward force (though true aerofoil analysis requires circulation theory for full accuracy)
  • Venturi meter: Flow speed determined by pressure difference across a constriction
  • Pitot tube: Aircraft airspeed measured by dynamic pressure ½ρv² = P_stagnation − P_static
  • Atomiser: Air blown across a tube opening creates low pressure → liquid rises and atomises

Reynolds Number — Laminar vs Turbulent Flow

Reynolds NumberRe = ρvL/η = vL/ν     [L = characteristic length, ν = kinematic viscosity]

Re is the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces. Low Re: viscous forces dominate → smooth laminar flow. High Re: inertial forces dominate → turbulent flow. Critical values for pipe flow: Re < 2300 (laminar), Re > 4000 (turbulent), 2300–4000 (transitional). Blood flow in aorta: Re ≈ 1000–4000 (near-transition). Air over aircraft wing at cruise: Re ≈ 10⁷ (fully turbulent).

Viscous Flow — Poiseuille's Law

For laminar flow of a viscous fluid through a cylindrical pipe (radius R, length L, pressure difference ΔP):

Poiseuille's LawQ = πR⁴ΔP / (8ηL)

The R⁴ dependence is the most striking feature: doubling the pipe radius increases flow rate 16-fold. Halving the radius reduces flow rate to 1/16 — why arterial narrowing (stenosis) dramatically reduces blood flow and requires only modest blockage to cause severe reduction in cardiac perfusion.

✈ Aerofoil Lift — The Full Picture

The common explanation "air goes faster over the top curved surface because it has farther to go, creating low pressure" is incorrect — it relies on the equal transit time fallacy (there is no reason parcels that split at the leading edge must meet at the trailing edge). The correct explanation involves the Kutta condition (flow must leave the trailing edge smoothly), the Kutta-Joukowski theorem (lift = ρvΓ where Γ is circulation), and the downwash of air (Newton's third law). Bernoulli applies, but the velocity difference is produced by circulation, not path length difference.
