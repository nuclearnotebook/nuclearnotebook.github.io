Friction — Essential Force for Motion

Friction is simultaneously an engineer's nuisance (wasted energy, wear) and an absolute necessity (walking, driving, gripping). Without friction, you could not walk, write, or pick up an object. A frictionless world would be chaotic — all objects would slide freely on all surfaces, unable to start or stop. Understanding friction quantitatively is essential for mechanical design, vehicle dynamics, sports biomechanics, and materials science.

Static Friction — Preventing Relative Motion

Static friction acts between surfaces in contact that are not sliding relative to each other. It can take any value from zero up to a maximum:

Static Friction Inequality0 ≤ f_s ≤ μ_s N     (μ_s = coefficient of static friction)

Static friction adjusts itself to exactly oppose the tendency to slide, up to its maximum value μ_sN. Push on a box with 5 N — static friction provides 5 N backward. Push with 10 N — static friction provides 10 N backward. Push with μ_sN + ε — the box starts to slide, transitioning to kinetic friction.

Kinetic Friction — Sliding Surfaces

Kinetic Frictionf_k = μ_k N     (approximately constant with speed at ordinary velocities)

Once sliding begins, kinetic friction is approximately constant regardless of speed (at ordinary velocities — at very high speeds, heating effects alter the coefficient). Kinetic friction is generally less than maximum static friction (μ_k < μ_s), which is why anti-lock braking systems (ABS) improve stopping: by preventing full wheel lockup, ABS keeps tyres at the static/kinetic transition where friction is greatest.

Microscopic Origin of Friction

At the microscopic level, "smooth" surfaces are rough — covered in asperities (microscopic protrusions). Friction arises from: (1) adhesion between asperities in contact (true area of contact ≪ geometric area), (2) plastic deformation and ploughing of softer material by harder asperities, and (3) elastic hysteresis in rubber. The empirical friction laws (Amontons' laws: f ∝ N, independent of apparent contact area and sliding speed) emerge from the statistical mechanics of these microscale contacts — the true contact area is proportional to N regardless of the apparent geometric area.

Air Drag — Fluid Resistance

Objects moving through air (or any fluid) experience a drag force opposing their motion. For fast-moving objects (Reynolds number Re > ~1000, turbulent flow):

Turbulent Drag (Newtonian drag)F_D = ½ρ C_D A v²     (directed opposite to velocity)

Where ρ is fluid density (air at sea level: 1.225 kg/m³), C_D is the dimensionless drag coefficient, A is the cross-sectional area perpendicular to motion, and v is speed. Key drag coefficients:

ObjectC_DNotes
Sphere0.47Baseline reference
Golf ball (with dimples)0.25–0.30Dimples cause turbulent boundary layer, reduce C_D by ~50%
Cyclist (racing position)0.88A ≈ 0.4 m², significant drag at speed
Toyota Prius0.25Industry standard aerodynamic design

Terminal Velocity

As a falling object accelerates, drag increases (∝v²) until drag equals gravity. At this terminal velocity, net force = 0 and acceleration = 0:

Terminal Velocityv_t = √(2mg / ρC_D A)

Human skydiver (spread-eagle position): m ≈ 80 kg, C_D ≈ 1.0, A ≈ 0.7 m²: v_t = √(2×80×9.81 / 1.225×1.0×0.7) ≈ 54 m/s ≈ 195 km/h. In a head-down "bullet" position (A ≈ 0.2 m²): v_t ≈ 100 m/s ≈ 360 km/h. A raindrop (r ≈ 2 mm): v_t ≈ 6–9 m/s (protected by small mass and large C_D in Stokes regime).

Stokes' Law — Low Reynolds Number Drag

For slow-moving small objects (Re < 1, laminar viscous flow — bacteria, dust particles, blood cells):

Stokes' Drag (Viscous Flow)F_D = 6πηrv     [η = dynamic viscosity of fluid, r = sphere radius]

Terminal velocity in Stokes' regime: v_t = 2r²(ρ_sphere − ρ_fluid)g / (9η). Millikan used this in his famous oil-drop experiment (1909) to measure the electron charge, observing droplets at terminal velocity in an electric field.

⛳ Why Golf Balls Have Dimples

A smooth golf ball would have C_D ≈ 0.47 (sphere). Dimples (typically 336–500) create a thin turbulent boundary layer that stays attached to the ball surface longer, delaying the separation of the wake. This reduces the size of the low-pressure wake behind the ball, cutting C_D to ~0.25 — nearly halving drag. A typical drive (~60 m/s) with a smooth ball would travel ~135 m; dimples extend this to ~230 m under the same conditions. The Magnus effect from backspin (causing aerodynamic lift) adds further distance.
