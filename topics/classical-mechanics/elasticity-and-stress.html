Elasticity — Physics of Deformable Solids

When forces are applied to a solid body, it deforms. If the deformation disappears when the force is removed, the behaviour is elastic. If permanent deformation remains, it is plastic. Understanding elastic behaviour is fundamental to engineering design: every structural component must remain in the elastic regime under normal loading. The physics of elasticity was systematically developed by Robert Hooke (1678), Augustin-Louis Cauchy (1820s), and Leonhard Euler, forming the mathematical foundation of structural engineering.

Stress and Strain — Intensive Properties

Engineering Stress and Strainσ = F/A     [Pa = N/m²]        ε = ΔL/L₀     [dimensionless]

Stress σ is the force per unit area within a material — it is intensive (independent of amount of material) and measured in pascals. Strain ε is the fractional deformation — also intensive. Using intensive properties allows material properties to be tabulated independently of specimen size: a rod twice as long under the same stress has twice the absolute elongation but the same strain.

Bulk Modulus and Shear Modulus

Bulk Modulus (Volumetric Compression)B = −P/(ΔV/V)     [Pa]     (all materials, including liquids)
Shear Modulus (Resistance to Shear)G = (F/A)/φ = τ/γ     [Pa]     (solids only — liquids G = 0)

The three moduli (E, B, G) are not independent: for isotropic materials, G = E/[2(1+ν)] and B = E/[3(1−2ν)], where ν is Poisson's ratio (lateral strain / axial strain when stretched axially; typically 0.25–0.35 for metals, ~0.5 for rubber — incompressible).

The Stress-Strain Curve

The stress-strain curve from a tensile test reveals the full material behaviour:

  • Elastic region (OA): Linear σ-ε relationship, Hooke's law holds. Slope = E. Fully reversible deformation.
  • Proportional limit (A): Last point of linear behaviour.
  • Elastic limit (A'): Last point of fully recoverable deformation.
  • Yield point (B): Onset of plastic (permanent) deformation. Yield strength σ_y defined here (or at 0.2% offset).
  • Plastic region (B→C): Permanent deformation accumulates. Strain hardening occurs.
  • Ultimate tensile strength (C): Maximum stress the material withstands. Necking begins.
  • Fracture (D): Material fails. True fracture stress > UTS because cross-section has necked down.

Elastic Potential Energy

Elastic Energy Stored in a Deformed BodyU = ½Eε²V = σ²V/(2E) = ½(F²/kA)     [energy density = ½Eε² J/m³]

Energy stored per unit volume = ½Eε² = ½σε. This is proportional to the area under the elastic portion of the σ-ε curve. For a spring: U = ½kx² where k = AE/L (spring constant derived from material and geometry).

⚠ Stress Concentration and Metal Fatigue

Stress concentrations at geometric discontinuities (holes, notches, sharp corners) amplify local stress by a factor K_t = 1–3 or more. Repeated cyclic loading at stresses well below the yield strength causes fatigue — microscopic cracks initiate at stress concentrations, grow with each cycle, and eventually cause sudden brittle fracture. The de Havilland Comet jet airliner crashes (1954) were caused by fatigue cracks at square windows; the subsequent redesign with oval windows (smaller K_t) corrected this, establishing modern aircraft fatigue analysis protocols.
