The Centre of Mass — A Point That Behaves Simply

The centre of mass (CM) is the unique point at which the entire mass of an extended body or system of particles can be considered concentrated for the purpose of analysing translational motion. For any system subject to external forces, Newton's Second Law applied to the CM gives a remarkably simple result: the CM accelerates as if all external forces acted on a single particle of mass M at that point.

Locating the Centre of Mass

CM of Discrete Systemr⃗_CM = (Σmᵢr⃗ᵢ) / M     M = Σmᵢ
CM of Continuous Bodyr⃗_CM = ∫r⃗ dm / M = ∫r⃗ ρ(r⃗) dV / M

For uniform bodies (constant density ρ), the CM coincides with the geometric centroid. For non-uniform bodies, the CM shifts toward the denser regions. The CM of a uniform rod is at its midpoint, uniform triangle at the centroid (intersection of medians, 1/3 from each side), uniform semicircle at 4R/(3π) from the diameter.

The CM Theorem — System Dynamics

Newton's Second Law for the CMF⃗_ext = M·a⃗_CM

Only external forces affect the CM motion. Internal forces (forces between parts of the system) always come in Newton's Third Law pairs: they sum to zero and cannot change the total momentum or CM motion. This has profound implications:

  • An exploding shell: the CM continues on the original parabolic trajectory (ignoring air resistance), even as fragments fly in all directions.
  • A rocket in deep space: the CM of (rocket + exhaust) moves at constant velocity (no external forces). The rocket accelerates because exhaust moves backward.
  • A diver: however complex the rotation, the CM traces a perfect parabola from board to water.

The Two-Body Problem

Two masses m₁ and m₂ interacting gravitationally — the exact problem of a planet orbiting a star. The CM moves at constant velocity (no external forces). Transforming to the CM frame and introducing the relative coordinate r = r₁ − r₂, the two-body problem reduces exactly to a one-body problem with reduced mass μ:

Reduced Massμ = m₁m₂ / (m₁+m₂)     [satisfies: 1/μ = 1/m₁ + 1/m₂]

The relative motion obeys: μ·(d²r/dt²) = −Gm₁m₂/r². This is equivalent to a single particle of mass μ orbiting a fixed mass (m₁+m₂). Kepler's laws apply to the relative orbit, but with M replaced by (m₁+m₂) in the period formula. For the Earth-Moon system, the CM is located ~4,671 km from Earth's centre (inside Earth's volume), and both bodies orbit this CM with the same period (27.3 days).

💡 The Fosbury Flop

In the high jump Fosbury Flop technique, the athlete arches their back as they pass over the bar. At the moment of clearance, every part of the athlete's body may be below the bar height — yet the CM passes over the bar. Since the CM must clear the bar height for the jump to succeed, arching the body so the CM is close to (or even below) the bar reduces the actual height the CM must reach, improving efficiency. Dick Fosbury used this to win the 1968 Olympic gold medal, and the technique is now universal.
