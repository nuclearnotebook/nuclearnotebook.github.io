The Conceptual Leap: Universal Gravitation

Before Newton, "gravity" was a terrestrial concept — it explained why heavy objects fell downward. Celestial motion was a completely separate phenomenon, governed by different (often circular) rules. Newton's extraordinary insight was that these are the same force. The gravitational pull of Earth that accelerates a falling apple also continuously deflects the Moon from straight-line motion into its orbit. This unification of terrestrial and celestial mechanics in a single mathematical law was the defining achievement of the Scientific Revolution.

Newton reasoned: the Moon is ~60 Earth radii from Earth's centre. The inverse-square law predicts gravity at the Moon's distance is 1/60² = 1/3600 of surface gravity. The Moon's centripetal acceleration toward Earth is (v²/r) where v = 2π×384,400,000/2,360,000 ≈ 1.02 m/s² × (1/3600) ≈ 0.00272 m/s². Earth's surface g = 9.81 m/s². Ratio: 9.81/0.00272 = 3607 ≈ 3600. The inverse-square law checked, to within observational accuracy. Newton had unified the heavens and Earth.

Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation

Newton's Law of Universal GravitationF = G·m₁·m₂ / r²     [directed along the line joining the masses, attractive]

Where: G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg² (the universal gravitational constant, measured by Cavendish in 1798), m₁ and m₂ are the masses in kg, and r is the centre-to-centre separation in metres. In vector form:

Gravitational Force VectorF⃗1 on 2 = −(Gm₁m₂/r²)r̂     (r̂ points from m₁ to m₂; negative = attractive)

Key properties of the gravitational force:

  • Always attractive: Unlike electromagnetic force, gravity has no repulsive counterpart. There is no "negative mass."
  • Infinite range: The 1/r² dependence means gravity weakens with distance but never reaches zero. Every mass in the universe exerts gravitational force on every other mass.
  • Acts on all matter: Every object with mass experiences and exerts gravity, without exception.
  • Weakest fundamental force: G is extraordinarily small. Gravitational attraction between two 1 kg masses separated by 1 m is only ~6.7 × 10⁻¹¹ N — about the weight of a bacterium. Electrostatic forces between charged particles are ~10³⁶ times stronger.

Gravitational Field

The gravitational field g⃗ at a point in space describes the gravitational force per unit mass that a test particle would experience at that location. It is a vector field — at every point in space, it has both magnitude and direction:

Gravitational Field Strengthg⃗ = F⃗/m = −GM/r² r̂     [N/kg = m/s²]

Near Earth's surface (r ≈ RE = 6.371 × 10⁶ m): g = GME/RE² = (6.674×10⁻¹¹ × 5.972×10²⁴) / (6.371×10⁶)² = 9.820 m/s². The slight variations (~0.05 m/s² across Earth's surface) reflect Earth's non-spherical shape, rotation, and variations in crustal density. Gravimeters measure these variations to locate subsurface oil deposits, mineral concentrations, and cavities.

Gravitational Potential Energy

The general gravitational potential energy of mass m at distance r from mass M, with U = 0 reference at infinity:

Gravitational Potential Energy (General)U(r) = −GMm/r     [U → 0 as r → ∞]

The negative sign reflects that work must be done against gravity to increase separation (move toward U = 0). The binding energy of a system (energy needed to completely separate it) is |U|. For Earth's gravitational field, the near-surface approximation U = mgh is derived by expanding U(RE+h) − U(RE) for h ≪ RE, giving U ≈ mgh where g = GME/RE².

Escape Velocity

Escape velocity is the minimum launch speed needed to escape a body's gravitational field entirely (reach r → ∞ with zero residual kinetic energy). Setting KE + PE = 0:

Escape Velocityvesc = √(2GM/R)     [independent of launch direction, mass of projectile]

Tidal Forces — The Differential Gravity

Tidal forces arise because the gravitational field strength varies with distance. The side of Earth facing the Moon experiences slightly stronger gravity than Earth's centre, which experiences stronger gravity than the far side. This differential — the tidal force — stretches Earth along the Earth-Moon axis and compresses it perpendicular to that axis. For a body of radius R at distance d from mass M, the tidal acceleration across the body's diameter is:

Tidal Accelerationatidal ≈ 2GMR/d³     [stretching along the line of centres]

Ocean tides (both the tidal bulge facing the Moon AND the bulge on the opposite side) are a direct consequence. The Sun also produces tides — about 46% as strong as the lunar tides. Spring tides (Sun and Moon aligned, new/full moon) add: tidal range ~2× average. Neap tides (Sun and Moon at 90°, quarter moon) partially cancel: tidal range ~0.5× average.

Gravitational vs Inertial Mass — Einstein's Starting Point

Newton's law uses mass in two distinct roles: gravitational mass (the "charge" that creates and responds to gravity, F = Gmg1mg2/r²) and inertial mass (resistance to acceleration, F = mia). Newton assumed they were equal; experiments by Eötvös (1889), Dicke (1964), and modern torsion balance experiments confirm mg/mi = 1 to 1 part in 10¹³. This equality is the empirical basis of Einstein's Equivalence Principle — the starting point for General Relativity.

🌌 Where Newton Fails: General Relativity

Newton's theory is extraordinarily accurate for everyday scales. It fails in strong gravitational fields and at high velocities. Mercury's perihelion precesses by 43 arcseconds per century more than Newtonian gravity predicts — explained perfectly by General Relativity. GR also predicts gravitational lensing (light bends around massive objects), gravitational time dilation (GPS satellites must correct for this), and gravitational waves (first detected by LIGO in 2015). Newton's gravity is the non-relativistic, weak-field limit of Einstein's GR.
