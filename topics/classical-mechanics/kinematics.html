Kinematics — The Language of Motion

Kinematics is the mathematical description of motion without reference to its causes. It provides the vocabulary — displacement, velocity, acceleration — and the equations relating them, which are used throughout all of classical mechanics. Kinematics is always the first step in any mechanics problem: describe the motion precisely before asking why it occurs.

Fundamental Kinematic Quantities

DefinitionsDisplacement: x⃗ = x_f − x_i     [vector, m]
Velocity: v⃗ = dx⃗/dt     [vector, m/s]
Acceleration: a⃗ = dv⃗/dt = d²x⃗/dt²     [vector, m/s²]

Critical distinctions:

  • Distance (scalar) ≠ Displacement (vector). Running around a 400 m track: distance = 400 m, displacement = 0 m.
  • Speed (scalar) ≠ Velocity (vector). Constant speed in a circle: speed = const, velocity is continuously changing direction.
  • Average velocity = total displacement / total time ≠ instantaneous velocity = derivative of position.

SUVAT Equations — Constant Acceleration

For the special but common case of constant (uniform) acceleration, the five SUVAT equations relate the five kinematic variables: s (displacement), u (initial velocity), v (final velocity), a (acceleration), t (time).

EquationVariables usedVariable absentDerivation
v = u + atv, u, a, tsDefinition of constant acceleration
s = ut + ½at²s, u, a, tvIntegrate v = u + at
v² = u² + 2asv, u, a, stEliminate t from first two equations
s = ½(u+v)ts, u, v, taAverage velocity × time (linear a)
s = vt − ½at²s, v, a, tuSubstitute first into second

Strategy: Identify 3 known variables and 1 unknown. Choose the equation containing those 4 variables (and lacking the fifth you don't need). For two unknowns, you need two equations — usually from two different directions in 2D problems.

Motion Graphs — Visual Kinematics

Position-time (x-t) graph: gradient = instantaneous velocity. Horizontal line = at rest. Upward slope = positive velocity. Curved line = changing velocity (acceleration present). The gradient of the tangent at any point = v at that instant.

Velocity-time (v-t) graph: gradient = instantaneous acceleration. Area under graph = displacement (signed area — area below the axis is negative displacement). Horizontal line = constant velocity (zero acceleration). Straight sloped line = constant acceleration.

Acceleration-time (a-t) graph: area under graph = change in velocity. Horizontal line at a = g = 9.81 m/s² for free fall.

2D Kinematics — Vector Component Method

For 2D motion, decompose all vectors into x and y components and apply kinematic equations independently to each direction. If the acceleration is constant:

2D Equations of Motionx:   vₓ = uₓ + aₓt    xₓ = uₓt + ½aₓt²
y:   vᵧ = uᵧ + aᵧt    y = uᵧt + ½aᵧt²

The x and y motions are completely independent — solving 2D kinematics reduces to two simultaneous 1D problems. The only quantity shared between the x and y equations is time t.

Non-Uniform Acceleration — Calculus Methods

When acceleration varies with time (or position), SUVAT equations do not apply. Integration is required:

General Kinematics (variable acceleration)v(t) = u + ∫a(t) dt     x(t) = x₀ + ∫v(t) dt

For acceleration as a function of position (common for spring forces, gravity at large distances): use the chain rule: a = dv/dt = (dv/dx)(dx/dt) = v(dv/dx), leading to ∫a dx = ∫v dv = ½v² − ½u². This is the derivation of the work-energy theorem from Newton's Second Law.

🏎 Formula 1 Performance Data

Modern F1 cars carry over 300 sensors generating 3 GB of data per race. Kinematics data (position, velocity, acceleration in 3 axes sampled at 1 kHz) is combined with tyre temperature, fuel load, and aerodynamic maps to optimise every aspect of car performance. Maximum longitudinal deceleration under braking: ~5g = 49 m/s². Maximum lateral acceleration in cornering: ~6g. Maximum rate of velocity change (jerk = da/dt): up to 1000 m/s³ — strong enough to cause whiplash in a road car occupant.
