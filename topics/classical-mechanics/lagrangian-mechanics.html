Why Reformulate Newton's Laws?

Newton's formulation of mechanics — forces, accelerations, vector equations — is physically transparent but often computationally cumbersome. For systems with constraints (a bead on a wire, a pendulum, a rigid body), constraint forces appear as unknowns that must be solved for. For systems with many degrees of freedom, the force equations become coupled and complex. Lagrangian mechanics eliminates constraint forces entirely and replaces vector force equations with elegant scalar equations derived from a single function — the Lagrangian. The power gain is enormous: the double pendulum, which requires pages of Newton's law algebra, is solved in a few lines with Lagrange.

Generalised Coordinates

The key concept in Lagrangian mechanics is the choice of generalised coordinates q_i — any set of independent parameters that completely specifies the system configuration. For an N-particle system in 3D with k holonomic constraints, there are n = 3N − k independent generalised coordinates. For a simple pendulum: 1 coordinate (angle θ) suffices — no need for x and y positions. For a double pendulum: 2 coordinates (θ₁, θ₂). For a particle on a sphere: 2 coordinates (θ, φ — the polar angles).

The Lagrangian

The Lagrangian FunctionL(q, q̇, t) = T(q, q̇, t) − V(q, t)     [kinetic energy minus potential energy]

L is a scalar function of the generalised coordinates q_i, their time derivatives q̇_i (generalised velocities), and possibly time t explicitly. Unlike the Hamiltonian (T + V = total energy), L = T − V is not the energy. Yet L contains all dynamical information about the system.

The Euler-Lagrange Equations

Euler-Lagrange Equation (for each q_i)d/dt(∂L/∂q̇ᵢ) − ∂L/∂qᵢ = 0     i = 1, 2, ..., n

One second-order ODE per generalised coordinate. Solving these yields the equations of motion — exactly equivalent to Newton's laws but often dramatically simpler to derive. The term ∂L/∂q̇ᵢ is the generalised momentum p_i. If L does not depend on q_i explicitly (q_i is "cyclic"), then p_i = ∂L/∂q̇ᵢ is conserved — an elegant automatic derivation of conservation laws.

Example: Double Pendulum

Using Newton's laws on the double pendulum requires resolving tension forces in two coupled vector equations — extremely tedious. With Lagrange: define θ₁ and θ₂ as the two angles. Write T₁ and T₂ (kinetic energies of each bob), V₁ and V₂ (gravitational PE). Form L = T − V. Apply Euler-Lagrange for θ₁ and θ₂. The constraint forces never appear. For small angles, the resulting linearised equations exhibit normal modes; for large angles, the motion is chaotic.

The Principle of Least Action

The Euler-Lagrange equations arise from a deeper variational principle. The action S is defined as:

ActionS = ∫t₁t₂ L(q, q̇, t) dt     [J·s = kg·m²/s]

Hamilton's Principle (Principle of Least/Stationary Action): the physical path taken by a system between two configurations is the one for which the action S is stationary (δS = 0) — not necessarily a minimum, but a saddle point or extremum. This variational principle is arguably the deepest statement of classical mechanics. It can be shown to be exactly equivalent to Newton's laws but extends naturally to quantum mechanics (Feynman's path integral), special relativity, general relativity, and quantum field theory. The action is Lorentz-invariant in relativistic theories.

Noether's Theorem — Symmetry and Conservation

Emmy Noether's theorem (1915) establishes the profound connection between symmetries and conservation laws:

💎 Noether's Theorem

Every continuous symmetry of the Lagrangian corresponds to a conserved quantity.

• Time-translation symmetry (L doesn't change with t) → Conservation of Energy
• Spatial-translation symmetry (L doesn't change with x, y, or z) → Conservation of Linear Momentum
• Rotational symmetry (L doesn't change with angle) → Conservation of Angular Momentum
• Gauge symmetry in electromagnetism → Conservation of Electric Charge

This is one of the most profound and beautiful results in theoretical physics — conserved quantities are not empirical accidents but necessities following from mathematical symmetries of the laws of nature.
