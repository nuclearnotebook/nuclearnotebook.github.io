What is a Wave?

A wave is a disturbance that propagates through space and time, transferring energy and momentum without transferring matter. This definition captures the essence: when a wave passes through a medium, the medium itself oscillates about its equilibrium position but does not travel with the wave. A Mexican wave in a stadium is a perfect human-scale illustration — each person momentarily stands up and sits down, but nobody moves along the stadium.

Wave Parameters and the Fundamental Relation

Fundamental Wave Relationv = fλ     [wave speed = frequency × wavelength]

The wave speed v is determined by the properties of the medium (tension and mass density for a string, elastic modulus and density for solids, pressure and density for sound). Frequency f is set by the source. Wavelength λ adjusts accordingly: λ = v/f. When a wave moves from one medium to another, frequency is preserved (set by the source), but v and λ change.

The Wave Equation

The wave equation governs how the displacement y of a medium element varies with position x and time t:

1D Wave Equation∂²y/∂t² = v² ∂²y/∂x²

General solutions: y = f(x−vt) (rightward-propagating wave) and y = g(x+vt) (leftward). The sinusoidal solution:

Sinusoidal Wavey(x,t) = A sin(kx − ωt + φ)     k = 2π/λ, ω = 2πf, v = ω/k

Transverse vs Longitudinal Waves

Transverse waves: displacement perpendicular to propagation direction. Waves on a string, electromagnetic waves, surface water waves (approximately). Can be polarised (displacement restricted to one plane).

Longitudinal waves: displacement parallel to propagation. Sound waves in air, P-waves in seismology, ultrasound. Cannot be polarised. Speed of sound in air (20°C): v = 343 m/s. Speed of sound in steel: ~5,100 m/s — 15× faster, because steel's bulk modulus far exceeds air.

Superposition Principle

For linear wave equations, the superposition principle holds: the total displacement when two or more waves overlap is the algebraic sum of the individual displacements:

Superpositiony_total(x,t) = y₁(x,t) + y₂(x,t) + ...

Constructive interference: crests coincide (phase difference 0, 2π, 4π...) → amplitude doubles. Destructive interference: crest coincides with trough (phase difference π, 3π...) → amplitude cancels. Intermediate phases give intermediate amplitudes.

Standing Waves — Resonance in Bounded Media

When two waves of equal amplitude and frequency travel in opposite directions (e.g., incident + reflected wave), they combine to form a standing wave — a pattern that oscillates in place with no net energy flow:

Standing Wavey = [2A sin(kx)] cos(ωt)

Nodes (y = 0 always): at kx = nπ → x = nλ/2. Antinodes (maximum oscillation): at kx = (n+½)π → x = (n+½)λ/2. For a string fixed at both ends (length L), boundary conditions require nodes at x = 0 and x = L:

String Resonant Frequencies (Harmonics)f_n = n·v/(2L) = n·f₁     n = 1, 2, 3, ...

The fundamental (n=1, f₁ = v/2L) is the lowest resonant frequency. Higher harmonics (overtones) at 2f₁, 3f₁, 4f₁... give musical instruments their characteristic timbre. A guitar string tuned to A₄ (440 Hz) vibrates with all harmonics simultaneously, the mix determined by how the string is plucked.

Wave Energy and Intensity

Wave IntensityI = P/A = ½ρvω²A²     (∝ amplitude²)

Intensity is proportional to the square of the amplitude — doubling amplitude quadruples intensity. For a point source radiating uniformly in 3D, intensity decreases as 1/r² (inverse-square law): I = P_source/(4πr²). This is why distant sources sound quiet and look dim — a direct consequence of geometry, not energy loss.

⚠ Tacoma Narrows Bridge Collapse (1940)

The Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapsed four months after opening due to aeroelastic flutter — a resonance phenomenon between the bridge's torsional oscillation mode and periodic vortex shedding from wind. The wind speed happened to match the bridge's natural torsional frequency, driving it into resonance. The collapse is a textbook example of the catastrophic consequences of resonance in engineering structures. Modern bridge design uses flutter analysis and tuned mass dampers to prevent resonance.
