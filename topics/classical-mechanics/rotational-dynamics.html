The Rotational Analogue of Newton's Laws

Every concept and equation in linear mechanics has a rotational counterpart. The systematic correspondence between linear and rotational quantities is one of the most elegant structures in classical mechanics. Rotational dynamics describes how objects spin, precess, and rotate under the influence of torques.

Torque — The Rotational Analogue of Force

Torque τ⃗ is the tendency of a force to cause rotation about an axis. It is the cross product of the position vector from the axis to the point of force application, and the force vector:

Torqueτ⃗ = r⃗ × F⃗     |τ| = rF sinφ = F·d⊥

Where φ is the angle between r⃗ and F⃗, and d⊥ = r sinφ is the "lever arm" — the perpendicular distance from the axis to the line of action of the force. Torque is maximised when force is perpendicular to r (φ = 90°). The SI unit of torque is the newton-metre (N·m).

Physical intuition: A longer wrench handle increases torque for the same applied force (larger r). Pushing a door at the hinged edge produces no torque (d⊥ = 0). A door is most easily opened by pushing far from the hinges and perpendicular to the door (maximising both r and sinφ).

Moment of Inertia — The Rotational Analogue of Mass

The moment of inertia I is the rotational inertia — the resistance to changes in angular velocity. It depends not just on mass, but on its distribution relative to the rotation axis:

Moment of InertiaI = Σmᵢrᵢ² = ∫r² dm     [kg·m²]
ObjectAxisMoment of Inertia
Point mass m at radius rThrough centreI = mr²
Thin ring, radius RCentral axisI = MR²
Solid disk/cylinder, radius RCentral axisI = ½MR²
Hollow cylinder, radii R₁, R₂Central axisI = ½M(R₁²+R₂²)
Solid sphere, radius RThrough centreI = ⅖MR²
Hollow sphere, radius RThrough centreI = ⅔MR²
Thin rod, length LThrough centreI = 1/12 ML²
Thin rod, length LThrough endI = ⅓ML²

The Parallel Axis Theorem relates the moment of inertia about any parallel axis to the CM value: I = ICM + Md², where d is the distance between the axes.

Newton's Second Law for Rotation

Rotational Newton's Second Lawτnet = Iα     [exactly analogous to Fnet = ma]

Angular acceleration α = dω/dt. If net torque is zero, angular velocity is constant (rotational equivalent of Newton's First Law). This is used in balancing rotating machinery — ensuring the net torque about the rotation axis is zero to prevent vibration.

Angular Momentum and Its Conservation

Angular MomentumL⃗ = Iω⃗ = r⃗ × p⃗     [kg·m²/s]

Angular momentum is conserved when no net external torque acts. This is one of the most important conservation laws in physics, with applications across all scales:

  • Figure skater: Pulling arms inward reduces I, increases ω to conserve L. The spin accelerates dramatically.
  • Forming stars: As a gas cloud collapses under gravity, its rotation rate increases enormously (L conserved, I decreases), forming rapidly spinning neutron stars and black holes.
  • Atomic physics: Electron orbital angular momentum is quantised in units of ℏ = h/2π, directly following from L conservation in spherically symmetric potentials.
  • Quantum mechanics: Angular momentum is a fundamental quantum number. Spin angular momentum (ℏ/2 for electrons) has no classical counterpart.

Rotational Kinetic Energy

Rotational Kinetic EnergyKErot = ½Iω²

For a rolling object (rolling without slipping), both translational and rotational KE contribute: KEtotal = ½mvCM² + ½Iω² = ½mv²(1 + I/mR²). A solid sphere (I = ⅖mR²) reaches the bottom of an incline with v = √(10gh/7), while a ring (I = mR²) reaches with v = √(gh). The more mass concentrated at the rim, the slower it rolls — energy goes into spinning the rim rather than translating the CM.

🌟 Neutron Stars and Angular Momentum

When a massive star collapses to a neutron star, its radius shrinks from ~10⁶ km to ~10 km — a factor of 10⁵. By L = Iω ∝ R²ω = constant, ω increases by 10¹⁰. A star that previously rotated once per month now spins 1,000 times per second — consistent with observed millisecond pulsars. The fastest known pulsar (PSR J1748-2446ad) rotates at 716 Hz.
