From Linear to Angular — A Shift in Perspective

Circular motion represents the most important departure from straight-line kinematics. It appears throughout nature — planetary orbits, electron paths in magnetic fields, the rotation of rigid bodies, the circular racetrack, and the operation of every rotating machine. To analyse circular motion efficiently, physicists introduce angular quantities that are the rotational analogues of linear displacement, velocity, and acceleration.

Angular Kinematics

Angular QuantitiesAngular displacement: θ (radians)    s = rθ
Angular velocity: ω = dθ/dt (rad/s)    v = rω
Angular acceleration: α = dω/dt (rad/s²)    at = rα

The radian is the natural unit for angle in physics — it is dimensionless (arc length / radius). One full revolution = 2π radians. The relationship v = rω is crucial: a point farther from the axis of rotation has a higher tangential speed for the same angular velocity. This is why the outer lanes of a running track are longer.

Centripetal Acceleration — Direction and Magnitude

The fundamental insight of circular motion kinematics: even at constant speed, a particle moving in a circle is accelerating — because its velocity direction continuously changes. This acceleration always points toward the centre of the circle:

Centripetal (Centre-Pointing) Accelerationac = v²/r = ω²r     (always directed inward)

The magnitude: for a particle of speed v at radius r. The direction: always perpendicular to v, pointing toward the centre (hence "centripetal" — Latin for "centre-seeking"). Note that centripetal acceleration changes the direction of v but not its magnitude.

Centripetal Force — What Provides It?

By Newton's Second Law, the centripetal acceleration requires a net inward force:

Centripetal Force RequirementFc = m·ac = mv²/r = mω²r     (directed inward)

"Centripetal force" is not a new type of force — it is a description of the role played by whatever force (or combination of forces) provides the inward acceleration:

  • Planetary/satellite orbit: Gravity provides Fc
  • Ball on a string: String tension provides Fc
  • Car rounding a curve: Friction from road provides Fc
  • Banked curve: Component of normal force provides Fc
  • Electron in magnetic field: Magnetic force (Lorentz force) provides Fc
  • LHC proton beam: Magnetic force from dipole magnets provides Fc

Banked Curves — Optimising for Speed

A road banked at angle θ to the horizontal allows vehicles to round a curve of radius r at speed v without relying on friction. The normal force N from the banked surface provides both a vertical component (balancing gravity) and a horizontal component (providing centripetal force):

Ideal Banking Angletan θ = v²/(rg)     (no friction needed at this speed)

For the Formula 1 Circuit of the Americas, the banked Turn 1 (r ≈ 85 m, designed for ~220 km/h = 61 m/s): θ = arctan(61²/(85×9.81)) = arctan(4.47) ≈ 77° — nearly vertical. In practice, banking is a compromise for a range of speeds.

Vertical Circles — Variable Speed

For a particle moving in a vertical circle (e.g., a roller coaster loop, a ball on a string), speed changes with height (energy conservation), so centripetal acceleration is not constant. At the top of a loop of radius r, the minimum speed to maintain contact requires:

Minimum Speed at Top of Vertical Loopvmin,top = √(rg)     (normal force N = 0 at minimum)

At the bottom of the same loop: N − mg = mv²/r → N = m(g + v²/r). The rider experiences apparent weight greater than real weight — the sensation of being "pressed into the seat" at g + v²/r times their mass.

Non-Uniform Circular Motion — Total Acceleration

When speed changes around a circular path (non-uniform circular motion), the total acceleration has two perpendicular components:

Total Acceleration — Non-Uniform Circular Motiona⃗total = a⃗c + a⃗t     |a⃗| = √(ac² + at²)
ac = v²/r (centripetal, inward)    at = dv/dt = rα (tangential)
⚛ Cyclotrons and Synchrotrons

Particle accelerators exploit circular motion physics. In a cyclotron, charged particles spiral outward in a magnetic field (providing centripetal force) while being accelerated by an electric field. The period T = 2πm/(qB) is independent of speed (non-relativistic), enabling resonant acceleration. In a synchrotron (like the LHC), the magnetic field is increased as particles gain energy to maintain a fixed circular radius — requiring precise synchronisation of all 1,232 dipole magnets.
