Statics — The Science of Balance

Statics is the branch of mechanics studying bodies in equilibrium — at rest or moving at constant velocity. Every building you enter, every bridge you cross, and every shelf you load relies on static equilibrium. The conditions for equilibrium are among the most practically important equations in physics.

The Two Conditions for Static Equilibrium

A rigid body is in static equilibrium if and only if both conditions are simultaneously satisfied:

Condition 1: Translational EquilibriumΣF⃗ = 0     (vector sum of all forces = zero)
Condition 2: Rotational EquilibriumΣτ⃗ = 0     (sum of all torques about any axis = zero)

In 2D: 3 independent equations (ΣFₓ=0, ΣFᵧ=0, Στ=0), allowing 3 unknowns to be determined. In 3D: 6 equations (3 force + 3 torque components). A structure with more unknown forces than equilibrium equations is statically indeterminate and requires additional deformation analysis.

Free Body Diagram Analysis

The systematic approach for any statics problem follows the same steps as dynamics, but with a = 0:

  1. Draw a clear free body diagram with ALL forces and their points of application
  2. Choose an axis for torque calculations — place it at an unknown force's location to eliminate that unknown
  3. Write ΣFₓ=0, ΣFᵧ=0, and Στ=0
  4. Solve the simultaneous equations for unknowns

Axis selection trick: For a beam with unknown reactions at supports A and B, placing the torque axis at A makes A's reaction contribute zero torque, leaving only B's reaction and the loads in the torque equation — solving directly for B.

Stability and Centre of Gravity

An object on a surface is stable as long as the vertical projection of its centre of gravity (CG) falls within the base of support — the convex hull of the contact area. When the CG projection moves outside the base of support, a tipping torque causes the object to fall.

Three types of equilibrium:

  • Stable: Any small displacement raises the CG → restoring torque returns it (ball in bowl, sitting person)
  • Unstable: Any small displacement lowers the CG → tipping torque amplifies displacement (pencil balanced on tip)
  • Neutral: CG height unchanged by displacement (ball on flat surface)

Truss Analysis — Method of Joints

A truss is a structure composed of two-force members (pin-jointed at each end) that carry only axial forces (tension or compression — no bending). At each pin joint, equilibrium requires ΣFₓ=0 and ΣFᵧ=0. Starting from a joint with only 2 unknowns, solve sequentially until all member forces are found.

📐 Why Triangles?

Triangles are the fundamental rigid shape in structural engineering. A triangle cannot deform without changing the length of at least one member — it is intrinsically rigid. A quadrilateral, by contrast, can be sheared (parallelogram deformation) without any member changing length. This is why trusses, bridge decks, and lattice towers are built from triangular units: triangulation provides rigidity using the minimum amount of material, minimising weight while maximising structural integrity.

Applications in Architecture and Biomechanics

The human body is a remarkably sophisticated static structure. Standing on one leg: the leg must support not just body weight (acting at the body's CG) but also the torque due to the body's CG being medial to the support foot. This torque must be balanced by the abductor muscle group on the opposite hip — a lever system where a small abductor moment arm requires large muscle force. This is why hip replacement patients must rehabilitate abductor strength before returning to walking.

🏛 The Pantheon — 2000 Years of Statics

Rome's Pantheon (completed ~125 CE) has a concrete dome 43.3 m in diameter that has stood for ~1900 years. Its genius is the progressive reduction of concrete density toward the top (using pumice aggregate instead of travertine), shifting the CG lower and reducing hoop tension stresses that would cause cracking. The oculus at the top is not structurally weak but reduces the dome's weight at precisely the point where tension stresses would be highest — a brilliant application of Roman empirical structural mechanics.
