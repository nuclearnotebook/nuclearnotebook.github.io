From Lagrange to Hamilton — A Transformation of Perspective

Hamiltonian mechanics, developed by Sir William Rowan Hamilton in the 1830s, reformulates classical mechanics in terms of coordinates and conjugate momenta — the "phase space" description. Rather than the n second-order differential equations of Lagrange, Hamilton provides 2n first-order equations. The resulting framework treats position and momentum as equals — a symmetry that becomes the correspondence principle in quantum mechanics and the foundation of statistical mechanics.

The Hamiltonian — Energy in Phase Space

The Hamiltonian H is defined by the Legendre transform of the Lagrangian:

The HamiltonianH(q, p, t) = Σi pᵢq̇ᵢ − L(q, q̇, t)     [pᵢ = ∂L/∂q̇ᵢ = canonical momentum]

For most systems (when T is quadratic in q̇ and V is independent of q̇): H = T + V = total mechanical energy. The Hamiltonian describes the system's energy as a function of coordinates (q) and momenta (p) — the phase space variables.

Hamilton's Equations of Motion

Hamilton's Canonical Equationsdqᵢ/dt = ∂H/∂pᵢ            dpᵢ/dt = −∂H/∂qᵢ

These 2n first-order equations completely determine the time evolution of the system. They are beautifully symmetric: the evolution of each coordinate q is determined by ∂H/∂p, and the evolution of each momentum p by −∂H/∂q. The minus sign ensures that H is conserved (dH/dt = 0) when H has no explicit time dependence.

Phase Space and Liouville's Theorem

Phase space is the 2n-dimensional space of all possible states (q₁,...,qₙ, p₁,...,pₙ). Each point represents a complete physical state. The system evolves by tracing a path through phase space. Key properties:

  • Phase space trajectories never cross (determinism: each state has a unique future)
  • For the harmonic oscillator: trajectories are concentric ellipses (x/A)² + (p/pmax)² = 1
  • For a free particle: trajectories are horizontal lines (p = const, x increases)
Liouville's Theoremdρ/dt = 0     (phase space density is conserved along trajectories)

The phase space volume occupied by any ensemble of systems is conserved in time under Hamiltonian evolution. This is the foundation of statistical mechanics: a thermal equilibrium distribution (microcanonical ensemble) has constant density on an energy surface.

Poisson Brackets

Poisson Bracket{f, g} = Σᵢ(∂f/∂qᵢ ∂g/∂pᵢ − ∂f/∂pᵢ ∂g/∂qᵢ)

The time evolution of any quantity f(q,p,t) is: df/dt = {f, H} + ∂f/∂t. A quantity is conserved if {f, H} = 0. The fundamental Poisson brackets are: {qᵢ, pⱼ} = δᵢⱼ (Kronecker delta), {qᵢ, qⱼ} = 0, {pᵢ, pⱼ} = 0.

The Bridge to Quantum Mechanics

Canonical quantisation replaces classical variables with quantum operators and Poisson brackets with commutators:

Canonical Quantisation RuleClassical: {A, B}  →  Quantum: [Â, B̂]/(iℏ)

Specifically: {x, p} = 1 → [x̂, p̂] = iℏ — the fundamental quantum commutation relation, from which Heisenberg's uncertainty principle follows: ΔxΔp ≥ ℏ/2. Hamilton's equations → Heisenberg's equations of motion in quantum mechanics. The Hamiltonian H becomes the quantum mechanical energy operator Ĥ, whose eigenvalues are the allowed energy levels.

🎲 Statistical Mechanics Connection

The Boltzmann distribution of statistical mechanics — assigning probability e^(−H/kT) to each microstate — is formulated in Hamiltonian phase space. Liouville's theorem guarantees that the microcanonical ensemble (uniform distribution on a constant-energy surface) is a valid equilibrium distribution. The partition function Z = ∫e^(−H/kT) dq dp is an integral over all of phase space — providing the bridge between mechanics (through H) and thermodynamics (through −kT ln Z = Helmholtz free energy).
