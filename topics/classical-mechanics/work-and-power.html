Work — The Formal Definition

In everyday language, "work" means physical effort. In physics, work has a precise definition that often conflicts with intuition: work is done on an object only when a force acts through a displacement. Holding a heavy weight stationary — however exhausting — does zero work in the physics sense. Pushing against a wall that doesn't move: zero work. A magnetic force on a moving charge moving in a circle: zero work (force always perpendicular to motion).

Work by a Constant ForceW = F⃗ · d⃗ = Fd cosθ     [J = N·m]

The dot product is essential: only the component of force along the displacement direction contributes to work. Signs: W > 0 if force has component in displacement direction (force aids motion); W < 0 if force opposes motion (friction, air drag); W = 0 if force is perpendicular to displacement.

Work by a Variable Force (1D)W = ∫x₁x₂ F(x) dx     (area under F-x graph)

For a spring (F = kx): W = ∫₀ˣ kx dx = ½kx² — the familiar elastic PE formula. For gravity near Earth's surface (constant F = mg): W = mgh. For gravitational force over large distances: W = GMm(1/r₁ − 1/r₂).

Power — Rate of Energy Transfer

Instantaneous PowerP = dW/dt = F⃗·v⃗ = Fv cosθ     [W = J/s]

Average power: Pavg = W/t = ΔE/t. The watt (W) is 1 J/s. The kilowatt-hour (kWh) — the unit on electricity bills — is energy: 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J = 3.6 MJ.

Simple Machines — Force Multipliers

A simple machine is any device that transmits force, changes its direction, or multiplies it. All simple machines obey the principle that work input equals work output (for ideal, frictionless machines): Win = Wout → Findin = Foutdout. A machine can multiply force or distance, never both. The Mechanical Advantage (MA) is the force ratio:

Efficiency — Real Machines

Mechanical Efficiencyη = Wuseful out/Wtotal in × 100% = Pout/Pin × 100%

Real machines always have η < 100% due to friction, deformation, and other dissipative losses. Typical values: bicycle chain drive ~98%, electric motor ~85–95%, car engine ~25–30%, steam turbine ~40–45%, human muscle ~25–30%. The "lost" energy is converted to heat — it doesn't disappear, but it's no longer mechanically useful (Second Law of Thermodynamics).

⚡ Regenerative Braking

Electric and hybrid vehicles use regenerative braking to recover kinetic energy that would otherwise be lost as heat. When braking, the electric motor runs in reverse as a generator, converting KE back to electrical energy stored in the battery. Modern systems recover 60–70% of the braking energy. A Tesla Model 3 recovers ~0.2 kWh per 10 km of city driving through regenerative braking — significantly extending effective range.
