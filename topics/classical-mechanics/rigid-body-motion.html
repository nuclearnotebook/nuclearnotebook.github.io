Rigid Bodies — Beyond Point Particles

A rigid body is an idealisation of a solid object whose shape is maintained exactly — no deformation under any applied force. Real bodies are approximately rigid when stresses are small compared to the elastic modulus. The key complexity is that a rigid body has rotational degrees of freedom in addition to translational ones, and its rotational inertia depends on the axis of rotation through the inertia tensor — a 3×3 symmetric matrix generalising the scalar moment of inertia.

The Inertia Tensor

For rotation about an arbitrary axis, the scalar I in L = Iω generalises to a matrix relation:

Inertia TensorIᵢⱼ = ∫ρ(r)(r²δᵢⱼ − rᵢrⱼ) dV     [3×3 symmetric matrix, kg·m²]

The diagonal elements are the familiar moments of inertia about the x, y, z axes. The off-diagonal "products of inertia" Iᵢⱼ (i≠j) represent the asymmetry of mass distribution. For L = Iω (matrix multiplication), L and ω are generally not parallel — this is the source of gyroscopic precession and the wobbling of asymmetric rotating bodies.

Principal Axes — Diagonalising the Tensor

Every rigid body has three mutually perpendicular principal axes (eigenvectors of the inertia tensor) about which the tensor is diagonal. In the principal axis frame: L = (I₁ω₁, I₂ω₂, I₃ω₃). Rotation about a principal axis is "clean" — L and ω are parallel and the motion is simple. Rotation about any other axis produces L ≠ ω, causing the characteristic wobbling of asymmetric objects.

Euler's Equations of Rigid Body Motion

In the body-fixed (principal axis) frame, Newton's law for rotation τ = dL/dt gives Euler's equations:

Euler's Equations (in principal axis frame)I₁(dω₁/dt) − (I₂−I₃)ω₂ω₃ = τ₁
I₂(dω₂/dt) − (I₃−I₁)ω₃ω₁ = τ₂
I₃(dω₃/dt) − (I₁−I₂)ω₁ω₂ = τ₃

For torque-free rotation (τ = 0), Euler's equations have exact analytic solutions for axially symmetric bodies (I₁ = I₂ ≠ I₃): free symmetric top, with body precessing around the angular momentum vector at rate Ω = ω₃(I₃−I₁)/I₁. For an asymmetric body with all three I values different, rotation about the intermediate principal axis (I₁ < I₂ < I₃) is unstable — the Dzhanibekov (tennis racket) effect.

Gyroscopic Precession

A spinning gyroscope subject to gravity precesses: the spin axis slowly rotates in a horizontal circle. The torque τ = Mgd (M = gyroscope mass, d = distance from pivot to CM) is horizontal. Since τ = dL/dt and L is along the spin axis, L changes direction — precessing around the vertical:

Precession Angular VelocityΩ_prec = τ/L = Mgd/(Iω)     (inversely proportional to spin rate ω)

At high spin rate (ω → ∞), precession becomes negligible — the gyroscope maintains its orientation. This is the principle of the gyrocompass: a rapidly spinning gyroscope seeks and maintains alignment with Earth's rotation axis, providing true north without relying on magnetic north.

Nutation and Real Gyroscope Motion

Superimposed on precession is nutation — a rapid bobbing of the spin axis. In the absence of friction, a gyroscope launched without perfect initial conditions nutates continuously. The nutation rate Ω_nut ≈ (I₃/I₁)ω — much faster than precession for compact gyroscopes. Earth itself precesses with a period of ~25,772 years (the "precession of the equinoxes," caused by the gravitational torque of the Sun and Moon on Earth's equatorial bulge) and nutates with a period of 18.6 years (caused by the Moon's orbital precession).

🚀 Spacecraft Attitude Control

Spacecraft use reaction wheels — flywheels driven by electric motors — to control orientation without using fuel. By spinning a reaction wheel in one direction, angular momentum conservation causes the spacecraft to rotate in the opposite direction (by Euler's equations and conservation of total L). Three orthogonal reaction wheels provide full 3-axis attitude control. The Hubble Space Telescope uses four reaction wheels (one redundant) to achieve pointing accuracy better than 0.007 arcseconds — essential for the long exposures needed for deep-field imaging.
